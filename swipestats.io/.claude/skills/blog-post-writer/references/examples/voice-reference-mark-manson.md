# Mark Manson Voice Patterns

SwipeStats' blog voice is modeled after Mark Manson's writing. This document captures the specific patterns that make his voice work, so you can replicate them.

## 1. Opens With a Gut Punch, Not a Setup

Manson never eases into a topic. He drops you into the deep end.

**Pattern:** First sentence is a strong claim, provocative question, or personal confession. No throat-clearing. No "In today's world..." No context-setting. Just the thing.

Examples from his work:
- "In my life, I have given a fuck about many people and many things. I have also not given a fuck about many people and many things. And those fucks I have not given have made all the difference."
- "Think about this for a moment: Why would you ever choose to be with someone who is not excited to be with you?"

**Anti-pattern (what NOT to do):**
- "Here's a number that should change how you think about your dating profile..." (too detached, too setup-y)
- "Understanding your Hinge prompts can help you..." (corporate blog energy)

## 2. Profanity as Punctuation

Manson uses profanity the way other writers use italics — for emphasis, rhythm, and intimacy. It signals "I'm talking to you like a friend, not writing a blog post."

**Pattern:** F-bombs land on the key insight or the punchline. Not random. Not every sentence. When the word "fuck" appears, it's doing work.

Examples:
- "Because when we give too many fucks, when we choose to give a fuck about everything, then we feel as though we are perpetually entitled to feel comfortable and happy at all times, that's when life fucks us."
- "No, screw that, mom. We're going to lawyer the fuck up and go after this asshole."

**SwipeStats equivalent:**
- "Your photos are doing 80% of the work. If your pictures look like they were taken by a gas station security camera in 2011, no prompt answer on Earth is saving you. Not Shakespeare. Not Ryan Gosling's speechwriter. Nobody."
- "If your analogy could be printed on a Forever 21 t-shirt, it's too generic. You're not a rollercoaster. You're a guy who can't think of a better fucking metaphor."

**Rule:** At least 2-3 instances of strong profanity per 1000 words. "Ass," "shit," "damn" more frequently. F-bombs on the landing spots.

## 3. Short Paragraphs as Punchlines

Manson uses single-sentence paragraphs like drum hits. After building up an idea, he lands it in one line. Then moves on.

**Pattern:**
- Build (2-3 sentence paragraph explaining something)
- Build more (another paragraph adding nuance)
- PUNCH (one sentence, its own paragraph)

**SwipeStats example:**
> "Sports, music, and food. You've just described every breathing human since the dawn of civilization."
>
> You might as well write "I am a person who exists."
>
> Get specific or don't bother.

## 4. The Extended Roast Analogy

This is the signature move. Take something bad (a weak prompt, a bad bio, a cliché) and compare it to something absurd. The analogy starts reasonable and escalates.

**Pattern:** "[Bad thing] is like [absurd comparison]. [Extend the comparison one step further than expected]."

**SwipeStats examples:**
- "That's a red flag in a trench coat pretending to be a green one."
- "Three funny prompts makes you look like the class clown who peaked in tenth grade. Three serious ones makes you look like you're interviewing for the position of boyfriend on LinkedIn."
- "'Good vibes and positive energy.' Do you know who else says that? Every MLM recruiter on Facebook. Every divorced guy's Bumble profile. Every wellness influencer selling overpriced mushroom coffee."

**Rule:** Every section that critiques a weak example MUST include at least one extended roast analogy. This is non-negotiable. It's the voice's signature move.

## 5. Self-Aware Meta-Humor

Manson acknowledges the absurdity of his own position. He's not above the situation — he's in it with you.

**SwipeStats examples:**
- "And yes, I realize the irony of me sitting here writing a blog post telling you how to be specific on a dating app. We're all just doing our best out here."
- "(You're reading a Hinge prompts article on a Tuesday. I'm writing one. We're the same species of unhinged.)"

**Rule:** Include at least one moment per article where the narrator breaks the fourth wall and acknowledges the absurdity of writing/reading dating app advice on the internet.

## 6. The "I" Narrator With Credentials

Manson doesn't hide behind "research shows" or "experts say." He leads with "I" and establishes credibility through experience, not authority.

**SwipeStats equivalent:** Paw Markus. "I've reviewed thousands of Hinge profiles." "I've personally swiped through hundreds of profiles that say 'travel the world.'" Not "SwipeStats data shows" (save that for specific stats). The default narrator is a person, not a brand.

**Rule:** The intro MUST establish Paw Markus by name. Not "we at SwipeStats" — "I'm Paw Markus, and I've [done the thing that gives me credibility]."

## 7. Empathy Through Brutality

The roasting works because it comes from care. Manson doesn't mock people from above — he mocks the universal human condition of being bad at something important.

**Pattern:** Roast → pivot to genuine help. Never just roast.

**SwipeStats example:**
- "Your photos get her to stop scrolling. Your prompts get her to type. Without both you're just a pretty picture she looked at while pooping (if you're lucky enough to be pretty)." → Then immediately gives actionable advice.

**Rule:** Every roast must be followed by (or embedded within) actual useful guidance. The ratio is roughly 40% roast, 60% help.

## 8. Sentence Rhythm: Short-Long-Short

Manson's paragraphs have a jazz-like rhythm. Short declarative. Longer explanation with a clause or two. Short punchline.

**Example pattern:**
"Bad prompts get you silent likes. Good hinge prompts get you replies. And replies are where dates come from."

Three sentences. 6 words, 7 words, 7 words. Punchy. Rhythmic. Done.

Compare to generic AI writing: "It's important to note that selecting the right prompts can significantly improve your match quality and lead to more meaningful conversations with potential partners." One sentence. 25 words. Dead on arrival.

## Summary: The Voice Checklist

Before finalizing any SwipeStats article, check:

- [ ] Does the intro establish Paw Markus by name?
- [ ] Is there profanity? (minimum 2-3 strong instances per 1000 words)
- [ ] Does every weak example get an extended roast analogy?
- [ ] Are there single-sentence paragraph punchlines?
- [ ] Is there at least one self-aware meta-humor moment?
- [ ] Does the narrator say "I" more than "we"?
- [ ] Is the sentence rhythm varied (short-long-short)?
- [ ] Does the article sound like a person at a bar, not a blog post on the internet?
