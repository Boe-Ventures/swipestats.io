## Why Your First Message Matters More Than Your Jawline

Let's get something straight. You matched. Congratulations. That means someone looked at your face, read your bio (maybe), and decided you weren't completely repulsive. That's the good news.

The bad news? That match means absolutely nothing if your first message on a dating app reads like it was generated by a broken chatbot from 2014.

Here's what our [SwipeStats data](/blog/tinder-statistics) from **7,000+ profiles, 294 million swipes, and 3.14 million matches** actually shows:

- **57% of Tinder conversations never progress past the first message.** More than half. Dead on arrival.
- **43.4% of male matches result in 0-1 messages total.** That's not a conversation. That's a cemetery.
- Only **14.95% of male matches become real conversations** (11+ messages exchanged). Less than one in seven.
- Only **2.09% of matches reach 50+ messages.** That's the "deep connection" tier, and almost nobody gets there.

OkCupid's data paints an equally grim picture: men need to send **18 messages** just for a 50% chance of getting a single reply. Women? They need five. Life isn't fair, and neither is online dating. Shocking.

The match is not the bottleneck. The message is. Your jawline got you through the door. Your first message determines whether anyone lets you sit down.

## The Three Rules That Separate Replies from Silence

Before I hand you copy-paste examples (I know that's why you're here, you lazy beautiful person), you need to understand why some messages work and most don't. Princeton researcher Susan Fiske found that people evaluate strangers on two dimensions: warmth and competence. Nail both and you get admiration. Miss both and you get... well, unmatched.

Here's how to start a conversation on a dating app without sounding like every other forgettable match in their inbox.

### Reference Something Specific (Don't Be the "Hey" Guy)

OkCupid analyzed millions of first messages and found that including the phrase "you mention" boosted response rates by **50%**. Fifty percent. Just by proving you read their profile.

There's a hierarchy here, and most guys are stuck at the bottom:

- **Bottom tier:** Generic compliment. "You're pretty." Cool, so is a sunset. She knows.
- **Mid tier:** Comment on a hobby. "I see you like hiking." Better. Still boring.
- **Top tier:** Personality trait they implied but didn't state directly. If her bio says she ran a half marathon and volunteers at an animal shelter, don't say "wow you're active." Say something about her being the kind of person who signs up for pain voluntarily. That shows you actually processed her profile instead of just scanning for keywords like a human search engine.

On Hinge specifically, comment on prompts over photos. The prompts exist to give you something to work with. Use them. That's like showing up to an open-book test and refusing to open the book.

For more on this, check out our guide on [starting conversations on Hinge](/blog/how-to-start-a-conversation-on-hinge).

### Ask a Question They Can't Ignore

A statement without a question is a dead end. "Nice dogs" is a compliment that goes nowhere. "Nice dogs. Think they'd get along with my cat or would it be a full-on turf war?" is a conversation.

The difference is that one requires a response and the other doesn't. You're giving them a reason to type back instead of hoping they'll generate momentum out of thin air.

Your questions need to be two things:

1. **Easy to answer.** Don't ask them to write an essay. "What's your life philosophy?" on a Tuesday night is a lot. "Would your dog judge my cat?" takes three seconds to respond to.
2. **Fun to answer.** If your question sounds like a job interview ("So what do you do for work?"), you've already lost. Nobody opens a dating app to fill out a questionnaire.

The best online dating first message combines a specific observation with a low-effort, high-fun question. That's the formula. Tattoo it on your forearm if you have to.

### Keep It Under Two Sentences

OkCupid analyzed over **500,000 first contacts** and found the sweet spot is **40-90 characters**. That's roughly one to two short sentences. Not a paragraph. Not a novel. Not your manifesto.

And yet the average first message clocks in at **743 characters**. That's eight to ten times too long. **16% of people send openers over 2,000 characters.** Two thousand characters. That's longer than most cover letters, and nobody enjoys reading those either.

Short messages work because they're low-pressure. They feel casual. They don't smell like someone who's been rehearsing in front of a mirror for 45 minutes (even if you have, no judgment).

Two sentences. One observation. One question. Send.

## First Messages for Tinder (That Don't Smell Like Desperation)

Tinder is a volume game. Bios are short, attention spans are shorter, and your message is competing with 47 other guys who all typed "hey" at 11pm while watching The Office for the ninth time. Your [best Tinder openers](/blog/best-tinder-openers) need to be fast, specific, and just interesting enough to earn a reply.

Use their photos, bio, or Spotify anthem as hooks. Here's what good looks like:

**If their bio mentions cooking:**
"Okay but what's the one dish you make that has people questioning whether you're secretly a professional chef?"

**If they have a travel photo:**
"That looks like [location]. Please tell me you actually tried the street food and didn't just eat at the hotel restaurant."

**If their anthem is something specific:**
"[Song name] as your anthem is a bold choice. Are you the kind of person who plays it at full volume with the windows down or are you a secret headphone listener?"

**If they have a dog in their photos:**
"Your dog looks like he has strong opinions about people. Would he approve of me or am I getting barked at on sight?"

**If their bio is funny or sarcastic:**
"Your bio made me actually laugh, which hasn't happened on this app since roughly 2019. That's either a compliment to you or very sad for me."

**If they mention a sport or fitness:**
"Alright, honest question. What's the most embarrassing thing that's happened to you at the gym? I need to know I'm not alone."

Every single one of these does the same three things: references something specific, asks a question, and stays under two sentences. Not complicated. Just requires you to actually look at their profile for more than half a second.

## First Messages for Hinge (Where the Prompt Does the Heavy Lifting)

Hinge is built differently. The prompts are your best friend here, and if you're ignoring them to comment on photos, you're playing the game wrong.

The data backs this up hard. Hinge's own research shows that **likes with comments are 2x more likely to lead to a date** than likes without comments. **72% of daters** say they're more likely to consider someone whose like includes a comment. You're literally leaving dates on the table by being lazy.

And here's a wildcard most people ignore: **voice notes**. Conversations that include a voice note are **40% more likely to lead to a date**. Hearing someone's actual voice builds trust faster than text ever could. It also proves you're not a bot, which is a higher bar than it should be in 2026.

For Hinge opening lines that work, comment on the prompt, not just the answer:

**If their prompt says "A life goal of mine is..."**
"Okay, [goal] is solid. But what's the backup plan if that doesn't work out? Because I feel like everyone needs a backup that's slightly unhinged."

**If their prompt is "I'm looking for someone who..."**
"I meet [X requirement] but I should warn you, I also [funny contradiction]. Package deal situation."

**If they have a "together we could..." prompt:**
"Together we could [playful spin on their answer]. But honestly I'd settle for you not being boring over text. The bar is underground at this point."

**If their prompt answer reveals a niche interest:**
"Wait, you're into [niche interest]? I have so many questions. Starting with: how did you even discover that?"

**If their prompt is about food:**
"[Their food take] is a brave stance. I respect it. But I need to know where you stand on pineapple on pizza before this goes any further."

**If they mention a controversial opinion:**
"[Their opinion] might be the hottest take I've seen on this app. I'm going to need your supporting evidence. Court is now in session."

## First Messages for Bumble (Where Women Make the First Move)

Bumble flips the script. Women message first, which means if you're a woman reading this (hello, rare creature who reads dating advice articles), your opener matters just as much.

The data says women who message first get **25% more responses** compared to the typical response rate for men's messages. The power is yours. Please don't waste it on "hey."

Bumble also has Opening Moves, where guys can set a pre-written question that auto-sends when someone matches. Think of it as your [Bumble opening move](/blog/bumble-opening-move) doing the heavy lifting while you're asleep.

**Tips for women sending the first message:**

- Don't just say "hey" and expect him to carry the conversation. That's the same low-effort energy you hate receiving. Be better than the thing you complain about.
- Reference his profile. Yes, men also want to feel like they're not interchangeable.
- Ask something specific. "I see you're into [hobby]. What's the most recent [related thing] you've done?" works on literally any profile.

**Tips for men setting Opening Moves:**

- Make it a question that's easy to respond to. Not "tell me your deepest fear." Something light. Something that feels like a conversation already happening.
- Humor lands well here. "On a scale of 1 to Gordon Ramsay, how would you rate your cooking?" gives them an easy on-ramp.
- Rotate your Opening Moves every couple weeks. Stale is stale.

## What NOT to Send (The Cringe Hall of Shame)

Look, I could give you a thousand great openers and it still wouldn't matter if you don't understand what [dry texting](/blog/dry-texting) looks like from the receiving end. So here's the official list of messages that make people hit unmatch faster than you can say "but I'm a nice guy."

**"Hey" / "Hi" / "What's up"**
An eHarmony survey found that **44% of daters are actively annoyed by lazy first messages**. "Hey" is not an opener. It's an admission that you couldn't be bothered. It's the first message equivalent of showing up to a job interview in pajama pants.

**Overly sexual openers**
Unless your bio explicitly says you're looking for something casual and theirs does too, leading with anything sexual is an instant unmatch for most people. Read the room. The room is a tiny phone screen and they don't know you yet.

**Generic compliments**
"You're beautiful" or "you have a great smile" tells them nothing except that you have functioning eyes. Specificity is attractive. "You're beautiful" is forgettable. "Your taste in [specific thing from profile] is elite" at least shows a pulse.

**Copy-pasted pickup lines**
They've seen it. All of them. Every single [pickup line](/blog/tinder-pickup-lines) that showed up on a Reddit thread in 2019 has been sent to them approximately four hundred times. If you didn't write it, they can tell.

**Interviewer mode**
"Where are you from? What do you do? Any siblings?" is not a conversation. It's a census. Space out the boring logistics questions. Lead with something interesting and get to the job-interview stuff later. Or never. Let that stuff come up naturally.

## FAQ

### Who should message first on Tinder?

Anyone can. There's no rule. Tinder gives both people 24 hours to message before the match can expire (depending on your settings). That said, if you matched with someone and you're just sitting there waiting for them to message you, congratulations, you're both doing the exact same thing and nobody is talking. Just send the message.

### How long should you wait to message a match?

Don't overthink this. Hinge's data shows that responses sent **within 24 hours are 72% more likely to lead to a date**. The "wait three days" rule is dead. It died with flip phones and MySpace. If you're interested, message them. If you wait too long, they've already forgotten who you are. That's not playing it cool. That's playing it stupid.

### Do women have to message first on Hinge?

No. Hinge lets anyone message first. But likes that include a comment are significantly more effective than blank likes. If you're going to like someone's prompt, write something. A bare like on Hinge is the equivalent of staring at someone across the bar and then just... walking away.

### What if they don't have a bio or their prompts are blank?

This happens. Some people treat their profile like a minimalist art installation. When there's nothing to reference, go with a hypothetical or situational question. "You didn't give me much to work with here, so I'm going to assume you're either a spy or terrible at bios. Which one?" Or ask something universal: "What's the last thing you watched that you couldn't shut up about?" You can also check our guide on [how to text a woman](/blog/how-to-text-a-woman) for more structured advice.

### My matches never reply. What am I doing wrong?

Probably one of three things. Your opener is too generic (see everything above). Your profile doesn't back up your message (a great opener from a terrible profile still gets ignored). Or you're waiting too long to message. Fix all three and watch your reply rate climb from "barren wasteland" to "actual human interaction."
