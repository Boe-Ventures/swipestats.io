## What Are Hinge Standouts? (And Why Is Everyone Obsessed With Them)

Hinge Standouts is the app's curated feed of profiles that are "catching the most attention, tailored to your preferences and recent activity." That's Hinge's official line. Translation: it's the popular kids' table, and you weren't invited to sit down for free.

You access Standouts by tapping the star icon at the bottom of the app. Every 24 hours, Hinge serves up roughly **10 new Standout profiles** picked just for you. These aren't random. They're the profiles pulling in the most likes and comments across the platform, filtered through whatever Hinge thinks your "type" is based on your swiping history.

The fundamental difference between Standouts and your regular Discover feed? Discover is essentially randomized based on your preferences (Hinge support has confirmed this). Standouts is algorithm-driven curation of the most in-demand people. Think of Discover as browsing the clearance rack at TJ Maxx. Standouts is the Gucci window display. You can look, but touching costs extra.

And by "extra," I mean a Rose. Which brings us to the part where Hinge reaches into your pocket.

## How Does the Hinge Standouts Algorithm Actually Work?

Here's something that legitimately surprised me when I dug into this. The Hinge Standouts algorithm is built on the **Gale-Shapley algorithm**, a stable matching formula that published in 1962 and later recognized with the Nobel Prize in Economics in 2012. The same math used to match medical residents to hospitals is now deciding which hot people show up on your phone while you eat cereal in your underwear. What a time to be alive.

The algorithm weighs two main factors. First, **profile engagement levels**: how many likes and comments a profile receives. Second, **your personal taste profile**: the patterns in who you've liked, passed on, and interacted with over time.

Hinge CEO Justin McLeod has specifically denied that there's a universal attractiveness score ranking everyone. Instead, the system builds individualized "taste profiles" for each user. So your Standouts feed looks different from your friend's Standouts feed. The algorithm isn't saying "these people are objectively hot." It's saying "these people are popular AND match your specific patterns." (Sure, there's a lot of overlap. But technically, it's personalized.)

The signals that feed into the algorithm include engagement volume, quality of engagement (comments weigh more than plain likes), photo performance, prompt quality, and your activity level. And here's a stat that should wake you up: **video prompts get 50% more engagement** according to Hinge's internal data. If you're still running six static photos and wondering why the [algorithm](/blog/hinge-algorithm) ignores you, there's your answer.

One more thing McLeod has said publicly: Standouts are weighted more toward "profiles you're likely to like" rather than "who's likely to like you back." Read that again. The algorithm is optimized to show you people you'll be attracted to, not people who'd be attracted to you. This is by design. It's also why Roses exist. But we'll get to that.

## The Rose Tax: How Hinge Makes Money Off Your Loneliness

Here's the deal. You cannot send a regular free like to anyone in Standouts. The only way to interact with a Standout profile is by sending a Rose. And Roses aren't free. Well, one is. Per week.

Let's do some quick math that'll make you want to throw your phone into a lake.

Hinge shows you approximately **10 Standouts per day**. That's roughly **70 per week**. You get **1 free Rose per week**. That free Rose doesn't roll over if you don't use it. So out of 70 curated profiles specifically chosen to appeal to your taste, you can interact with exactly one. The other 69? Pay up or shut up.

This is what the dating community calls **"Rose jail."** It's the number one complaint about Standouts and honestly, it's hard to argue with the frustration.

[Rose pricing](/blog/hinge-roses) varies by region, but expect something like **3 Roses for ~$3.99** or **12 for ~$29.99**. And here's the kicker that really stings: even [Hinge+ and HingeX subscribers](/blog/hinge-x-vs-hinge-plus) still need to buy Roses separately. Your $50/month subscription doesn't unlock free access to Standouts. Subscribers may get a few bonus Roses as a perk, but you're still playing the same rigged carnival game. Just with slightly better odds.

The one genuine upside of sending a Rose? It pushes your profile to the **top of the recipient's Likes You queue**, displayed with a little rose icon. So they'll actually see you instead of drowning in their 47 other pending likes. Whether they care is a different conversation.

## Are Hinge Roses Actually Worth Sending to Standouts?

Hinge's official claim: Roses are **2x more effective at getting you on a date** compared to regular likes. And if you add a comment with your Rose, that jumps to **3x more likely to get a response**.

Sounds great until you remember one thing. Every Standout profile is getting bombarded with Roses from everyone. You're not the only person who saw that attractive human with the clever prompt about their sourdough starter. You're competing against the entire user base of your area, all armed with their single weekly Rose or a fistful of purchased ones.

So the question isn't "do Roses work?" The question is "do Roses work well enough to justify the cost when you're one of fifty people sending one to the same profile?"

The strategic play, and I genuinely believe this, is to save your free Rose for someone in Standouts who actually matches your interests. Not just the hottest face. The person whose prompts made you laugh. The person whose bio references something you're actually into. A Rose with a thoughtful comment on a specific prompt is worth ten Roses sent to someone just because they were attractive. The 3x multiplier from adding a comment is real, and it's your biggest advantage.

There's a popular dating coach with 548K YouTube subscribers who says he never sends Roses because it feels "try-hard." I get his point. But I also think there's a difference between desperately Rose-spamming every attractive person and strategically sending one Rose per week with a killer [opening line](/blog/hinge-opening-lines) that shows genuine interest. One is desperate. The other is just playing smart.

One more thing: some Standout profiles may eventually appear in your regular Discover feed, where you can like them for free. But "may eventually" is doing a lot of heavy lifting in that sentence. The daily refresh means missed Standout profiles are essentially gone. Don't build a strategy around hope.

## How to Know If You're a Hinge Standout (Spoiler: There's No Badge)

Bad news for your ego: Hinge doesn't send you a notification saying "Congrats, you're a Standout!" There's no badge. No confetti animation. No little crown on your profile. You're a Standout and you don't even get to enjoy it. Classic.

The indirect signals that you're appearing in other people's Standouts feeds:

- You're **receiving Roses from other users** (the clearest indicator)
- Your match rate is unusually high
- Matches specifically mention finding you in their Standouts

Dating experts suggest a healthy Hinge account sees about **5+ inbound likes per day** and **1-2 inbound Roses per month** as a baseline. If you're getting significantly more Roses than that, you're almost certainly popping up in Standouts feeds regularly.

And here's a fun detail. If the same person keeps appearing in YOUR Standouts, that's Hinge telling you it thinks you two are a strong compatibility match. It's a signal, not a coincidence. Whether you want to burn your one weekly Rose on that signal is your call.

## How to Become a Standout on Hinge (Without Selling Your Soul)

Want the algorithm to put you in front of more eyeballs? Cool. Here's what actually moves the needle, based on the signals we know the algorithm tracks.

### Photos That Generate Engagement

Your photos don't just need to look good. They need to make people stop scrolling and actually interact. There's a difference.

Action shots, genuine smiles, varied settings. Show your life, not just your face. If your photos look like a security camera caught you committing a misdemeanor in 2011, no algorithm on Earth is saving you. Get a friend with a decent phone. Go outside. Smile like you've experienced joy at least once. Check out our [profile examples](/blog/hinge-profile-examples) if you need a template for not being terrible.

### Prompts That Start Conversations

Your prompts are conversation starters, not tombstone inscriptions. "I love to laugh" tells me nothing except that you've never had an original thought. Be specific. Be funny. Give someone something to respond to.

"The hallmark of a good prompt is that someone can read it and immediately know what to say back to you." If your prompt kills the conversation before it starts, you've written a bad prompt. Period.

### Video Prompts (The Cheat Code Nobody Uses)

I already mentioned this but it bears repeating: **video prompts get 50% more engagement**. Fifty percent. That's a massive edge and most people completely ignore it because recording a video of themselves feels awkward. Good. Let the competition stay uncomfortable. Your willingness to do something slightly cringe gives you a real algorithmic advantage.

### Activity Patterns That Don't Scream Desperation

Open the app daily. Spend 30 to 60 minutes. Send thoughtful likes and comments. Then close the app and live your actual life. Hinge rewards consistent users, not people who spend four hours rage-swiping at 2 AM because they're lonely and their ex just posted a story with someone new. (We've all been there. But the algorithm doesn't care about your pain.)

Be selective with your likes too. Mass-liking everything tells the algorithm your "type" is literally anyone with a pulse, which degrades the quality of your curation. And sending thoughtful comments instead of generic likes signals to the algorithm that you're a high-value user worth featuring. Quality of engagement matters as much as quantity.

## Standouts vs Most Compatible vs Enhanced Recommendations: The Hinge Feature Thunderdome

Hinge has multiple algorithmic features that sound similar but work completely differently. Here's the breakdown so you can stop confusing them.

**Standouts** = Popular profiles you'd probably like, based on engagement data plus your taste profile. Rose required. Roughly 10 per day.

**Most Compatible** = One daily profile that the algorithm thinks is your strongest compatibility match. Free like. This uses a completely different algorithm from Standouts and is optimized for mutual matching, not just one-sided attraction.

**Enhanced Recommendations** = Algorithm-suggested profiles that appear within your Discover feed, available to paid subscribers. No Rose required.

| Feature | Cost | Daily Profiles | Algorithm Focus |
|---|---|---|---|
| Standouts | Rose required | ~10 | Popular + your taste |
| Most Compatible | Free like | 1 | Mutual compatibility |
| Enhanced Recommendations | Subscription | Varies | Algorithm suggestions |

The key difference: Standouts shows you people you'll be attracted to. Most Compatible shows you people you're likely to actually match with. If you only have one free Rose, your Most Compatible might honestly be a better investment of your attention.

## Do Hinge Standouts Ever Show Up in Your Regular Feed?

The official answer: sometimes. The practical answer: don't count on it.

Hinge has confirmed that Standout profiles can occasionally appear in your regular Discover feed. But remember what Hinge support said: the regular feed is "basically randomized on your preferences" while Standouts is actively algorithm-driven. The chance of a specific Standout organically surfacing in your randomized feed is low.

Some people try the "wait and skip" strategy. They see someone in Standouts, don't send a Rose, and hope that profile shows up in Discover later. This almost never works. The Standouts feed refreshes every 24 hours, and missed profiles get cycled out. You're gambling your shot at connecting with someone on a random-number generator. If you've found someone genuinely compelling in Standouts, send the damn Rose.

CEO McLeod has explained that Standouts is weighted toward "profiles you're likely to like" rather than being optimized for mutual matching. That's exactly why the Rose paywall exists. Hinge knows you'll want to interact with these profiles. That's the whole business model.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Hinge Standouts

### Are Hinge Standouts mutual?

No. Just because someone appears in your Standouts doesn't mean you appear in theirs. Standouts are personalized to your taste profile. You might be in their feed. You might not. Hinge doesn't care about your feelings on this matter.

### How many Standouts do you get per day?

Roughly **10 profiles per day**, refreshing every 24 hours. That's about 70 per week that you get to stare at longingly while clutching your single free Rose.

### When do Hinge Standouts refresh?

Every 24 hours. Once they're gone, they're gone. No "recently viewed" tab. No second chances. (Sort of like dating in general, if you think about it.)

### Do Roses carry over week to week?

No. Your free [weekly Rose](/blog/when-do-hinge-roses-reset) is use-it-or-lose-it. If you don't send it, it vanishes into the void. Purchased Roses stick around, but the freebie has no rollover.

### Does Hinge X give you more Roses for Standouts?

[HingeX](/blog/is-hinge-x-worth-it) subscribers may receive additional Roses as a perk. But the Standouts system still requires Roses to interact. A subscription doesn't give you free access to the Standouts feed. You're still paying for Roses on top of your monthly subscription. I know.

### Can you see who sent you a Rose?

Yes. Roses appear at the **top of your Likes You queue** with a rose icon. So if someone sends you one, you'll see it before regular likes. That's the main tactical advantage of sending a Rose. Visibility, not desperation.
