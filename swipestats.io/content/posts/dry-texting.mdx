## What Is Dry Texting? (And Why Your Match Texts Like a Hostage)

Dry texting is when someone responds to your messages with the enthusiasm of a DMV employee on a Friday afternoon. We're talking one-word replies, zero follow-up questions, and the kind of conversational energy that makes watching paint dry feel thrilling by comparison.

Here's what it looks like in practice.

**Dry texting (the conversational death spiral):**

> You: "That hiking photo is amazing! Where was that taken?"
>
> Them: "Colorado"
>
> You: "Nice! Do you hike a lot?"
>
> Them: "Sometimes"
>
> You: (considers throwing phone into the ocean)

**Not dry texting (an actual human being engaging):**

> You: "That hiking photo is amazing! Where was that taken?"
>
> Them: "Rocky Mountain National Park! I went last summer with some friends and almost got chased by an elk lol. Do you hike?"

See the difference? One person is participating in a conversation. The other is holding their words hostage and charging ransom per syllable.

And it's not just annoying. A 2025 study from the International Journal of Creative Research Thoughts found that dry texting is directly linked to **lower relationship security** and trust. The study had a Cronbach's alpha of .93, which for those of you who slept through statistics class (no judgment, I did too), means the results are extremely reliable. Dry texting doesn't just kill conversations. It kills the emotional foundation underneath them.

## Why People Dry Text on Dating Apps (Spoiler: It's Usually Not Good)

Let's run through the reasons someone might be texting you like they're rationing vowels during a national shortage.

### They're just not that into you

This is the answer you don't want to hear, which is exactly why I'm saying it first. Most dry texting on dating apps is plain old disinterest wrapped in a thin veneer of politeness. They matched with you. They looked at your profile for 0.3 seconds. Now they're replying out of obligation, not attraction. You're getting the "MPI" treatment. That's Matthew Hussey's term for Minimum Possible Investment. Just enough effort to keep you on the hook without them actually having to do anything. Just enough to not feel rude. Just enough to waste your Tuesday evening.

### They're juggling 10 other conversations

Dating apps turn texting into a competitive sport, and your match might have a full roster. When you're replying to a dozen people at once, everyone gets the discount version of your personality. It's not personal. It's just math. Bad, soul-crushing math.

### They're genuinely bad at texting (rare but real)

Some people really do communicate better in person. These are the people who text "ok" to everything but then show up to a date and talk your ear off for three hours. They exist. They're just not as common as people on the internet would like you to believe. If someone texts you like a telegram operator but actually initiates plans and shows up with energy in person, you might have found a unicorn. Don't let them go. But also maybe teach them about sentences.

### Dating app fatigue has eaten their soul

**79% of Gen Z** report feeling drained by the swipe-match-ghost cycle. And it's not just the young ones. **80% of Millennials** feel the same way. When **65% of dating apps** get deleted within a month of download (rising to 69% in 2025), you know the whole ecosystem is running on fumes. Your match might not be dry texting you specifically. They might be dry texting existence itself.

## What the Data Says About Dry Texting (It's Worse Than You Think)

Time to hit you with some numbers. Because nothing validates your frustration like cold, hard data confirming that yes, most people on dating apps text like they're being charged per character.

A massive arXiv study analyzed **2 million conversations from 400,000 users** on a major dating platform. Here's what they found.

- **Average first message: 8 words.** That's it. Eight words to make a first impression. Most people are out here writing opening lines shorter than a fortune cookie.
- **Successful conversations averaged 30 messages.** Dead-end ones? Just 11. That's **3x more engagement** in conversations that actually went somewhere.
- **Men initiate 79% of conversations.** Women respond to only **16%** of the messages they receive. So if you're a guy and she's actually replying at all, that's already better than most. Even if it's just "lol."
- **52% of users** judge potential matches based on response speed. Meanwhile, **75%** worry about responding too quickly. So everyone's playing the same stupid timing game and nobody's winning.
- **62%** of people stop replying altogether over grammar and spelling issues. Your "your" vs. "you're" confusion isn't a minor thing. It's a deal-breaker for over half the dating pool.
- Including the word **"you"** in a first message raises response likelihood by **50%**. Because it turns out, people like knowing you're talking to them specifically and not copy-pasting the same message to everyone (even though you absolutely are).

And from our own SwipeStats dataset of **7,000+ real profiles, 294 million total swipes, and 3.14 million matches**: the average male right-swipe rate is **53%**. Guys are swiping right on more than half of every profile they see. They're casting a wide net and then wondering why the conversations feel shallow. The matches are there. The [actual conversations](/blog/how-to-start-a-conversation-on-tinder)? Not so much.

## Is Dry Texting a Red Flag? (The Honest Answer)

Here's a quote I want you to tattoo on the inside of your eyelids: **"If someone likes you, you'll know. If they don't, you'll be confused."**

Confusion is the answer. If you're reading this article because you're trying to decode whether someone's one-word replies mean they're interested, they're not. Interested people don't make you guess. They text you back with full sentences because they actually want to talk to you. Wild concept, I know.

### Signs it's disinterest (and you should stop making excuses for them):

- They never initiate conversations. You're always the one starting. Always.
- They take hours or days to reply but are posting Instagram stories in real time. They have the time. They just don't have the interest.
- They never ask you questions. Every message is a dead end you have to resuscitate with another question.
- Their replies are getting shorter over time. The trajectory is heading toward zero, and zero means ghosted.

### Signs they're genuinely just bad at texting (and might be worth keeping):

- They text short but they make actual plans. "Free Saturday?" beats a paragraph of nothing any day.
- They're enthusiastic and engaged in person. The contrast between their texts and their face-to-face energy is dramatic.
- They initiate hangouts even if they don't initiate texts. Actions over words. Always.

The IJCRT study put data behind what we all already knew: dry texting is directly correlated with **lower trust and emotional security** in relationships. It's not just "oh they're bad at texting." It actively erodes the foundation of connection.

And here's the fun part. **84% of Gen Z and Millennials have been ghosted.** Dry texting is just the slow-motion prequel to ghosting. It's the horror movie where the music is building and you're screaming at the character to get out of the house, but instead they send another "haha what do you mean?" text into the void. If your [match disappears](/blog/tinder-match-disappeared) after a string of dry texts, you saw the trailer. You just didn't want to believe it.

## How to Respond to Dry Texts (Without Losing Your Dignity)

Alright. You're getting dry-texted. Your pride is bruised. Your thumbs are tired from carrying the entire conversation. Here are your options, ranked from "worth trying" to "self-respect demands it."

### Stop mirroring their energy

Your instinct when someone sends you "lol" is to match that energy and send back something equally low-effort. Don't. Matthew Hussey's advice here is solid: model the energy you want, don't match the energy you're getting. Send something engaging. If they still come back with sawdust, at least you know you tried.

### Ask open-ended questions (real ones, not "how are you")

"How are you?" is a conversation coffin. Nobody in the history of dating apps has ever given an interesting answer to "how are you?" You know what they say? "Good, you?" And then you die a little inside.

Try questions that actually require a thought. "What's the most unhinged thing you've done this month?" "If you could only eat one cuisine for the rest of your life, what and why?" Give them something worth responding to. Check our guides on the [best Hinge openers](/blog/best-hinge-openers) or [Tinder openers](/blog/best-tinder-openers) for more ideas that don't suck.

### Playful accusations

This one comes from a YouTube creator named Hamza (the video hit 1.4 million views, so clearly it resonates). Instead of asking a question, throw out something absurd and accusatory: "I bet you're the kind of person who steps on snails for fun." It's weird enough to jolt someone out of autopilot texting mode. Does it always work? No. But it's more fun than asking "what do you do for fun" for the fourteen thousandth time.

### Voice notes

Here's a tip almost nobody uses on dating apps. Send a voice note. The guys over at TrueCam found that almost nobody does this, which means it immediately sets you apart from the wall of text everyone else is sending. Your voice conveys warmth, humor, and personality in ways that typed words can't. Plus, it's harder to "k" someone who just sent you a genuine voice message. Harder. Not impossible. Some people are truly dedicated to being terrible.

### The direct call-out

Sometimes the best move is just to name what's happening. Something like: "Hey, I've noticed these short exchanges aren't really going anywhere. Are you actually interested in chatting, or should we call it?" It's direct. It's respectful. And it gives them a clear exit if they want one (which, honestly, they probably do). This saves both of you from the slow bleed of a conversation that's already flatlined.

### Walk away

Ask yourself one question: "Does texting this person inspire me or tire me?" If the answer is the second one, stop. You are not a conversation paramedic. You are not obligated to perform CPR on a chat that somebody else killed. Close the app. Go outside. [Learn to flirt over text](/blog/how-to-flirt-over-text). Your time is the most expensive thing you own, and you're spending it on someone who can't even give you a complete sentence.

## How to Stop Being a Dry Texter (Yes, It Might Be You)

Plot twist. Maybe you're the dry texter. Maybe you clicked this article because someone finally called you out and you're here looking for a defense. You won't find one. But you will find a fix.

### Audit your own sent messages

Open your dating app right now. Scroll through your last five conversations. Count how many times you replied with one word or failed to ask a single question. If the number is higher than zero, you're part of the problem. Congratulations, self-awareness is the first step toward not being insufferable.

### Personalize every single message

Remember that stat about including "you" in a message raising response rates by **50%**? That's because generic messages feel generic. "Nice profile" could be sent to literally anyone. "You have a golden retriever AND you're into rock climbing? Either you're incredibly active or that dog is." That could only be sent to one person. Be specific. Reference their photos, their prompts, their [bio](/blog/best-tinder-bios). Prove you're a human being who actually looked.

### Ask questions, share stories, show up

A conversation is two people taking turns being interesting. Not one person interviewing and the other giving press conference answers. Share something about yourself. Tell a short story. React to what they said. The bar is so unbelievably low on dating apps that just being a functional conversationalist puts you in the top 20%.

### Use emoji with intention

I'm not saying carpet-bomb every message with the laughing-crying face. I'm saying a well-placed emoji adds warmth that plain text can't. It signals tone. It signals you're not a robot. One or two per message. Not twelve. We're going for "warm human" not "your aunt's Facebook comments."

### Move the conversation somewhere real

The best cure for dry texting is to stop texting entirely and start talking. Suggest a phone call. Send a voice note. Or (radical idea) ask them on an actual date. The whole point of dating apps is to stop being on dating apps. Every conversation that lives and dies inside the app is a conversation that never had a chance. If you want to know whether there's chemistry, you need to get off the screen and into the same room. Or at least the same phone call.

## FAQ: Dry Texting Questions That Keep You Up at 2 AM

### What does dry texting mean?

Dry texting means someone is replying with minimal effort. One-word answers, no follow-up questions, no emojis, no personality. It's the texting equivalent of someone showing up to a conversation, standing in the corner with their arms crossed, and making you do all the work. The [dating app statistics](/blog/dating-app-statistics) paint a pretty clear picture of how common this is.

### Is dry texting rude?

Technically? No. Nobody owes you a paragraph. But functionally? Yeah, kind of. If someone matched with you, they agreed to a conversation. Responding with "k" is like accepting a dinner invitation and then sitting at the table refusing to eat. You're there, but you're not really THERE.

### Why is he dry texting me all of a sudden?

Three possibilities, ranked by likelihood. One: he's losing interest and doesn't have the guts to say so. Two: something in his life pulled his attention away (new job, family stuff, another match he's more interested in). Three: he was never a great texter and you're just now noticing because the new-match excitement has worn off. The data says most dating app conversations die around message 11. If you're past that and the energy is dropping, you're probably watching the natural death of a conversation that wasn't meant to survive.

### Does dry texting mean they're not interested?

Usually, yes. Not always. But usually. The clearest signal isn't any single dry text. It's the pattern. If they're consistently low-effort across multiple conversations over multiple days, that's not a bad texting day. That's a person telling you with their behavior what they won't say with their words. Believe the behavior.

### How do you fix a dry text conversation?

You can try the strategies in this article. Open-ended questions, playful accusations, voice notes, the direct call-out. But here's the thing nobody wants to hear: you can't fix a conversation someone else doesn't want to have. You can improve YOUR texting. You can't force theirs. If you've made three genuine attempts to spark something and gotten nothing back, that's not a fixable problem. That's a [dead conversation](/blog/how-to-start-a-conversation-on-hinge) you need to walk away from and a new one you need to start.
