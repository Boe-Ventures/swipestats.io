## Why Your Conversations Keep Dying (Spoiler: It's You)

Let's get the uncomfortable truth out of the way first. If every conversation you start dies within three messages, the common denominator isn't "bad luck" or "people on this app suck." The common denominator is you.

Here's what the data says about how to keep a conversation going over text (or more accurately, how badly most people fail at it):

**Only 30-35% of matches ever send a first message.** That means two-thirds of you swipe right, get a match, and then just... sit there. Waiting. For what? A sign from God? An engraved invitation? Of those who do manage to type words into a text box, only **15-20% turn into actual back-and-forth conversations.** The rest die on arrival.

The average dating app conversation? **11 days. 15 messages. 11 words per message.** According to academic research from arxiv, that's the entirety of what most people manage before the silence swallows them whole. For reference, that's roughly the depth of conversation you'd have with a cashier at a gas station.

And timing matters. If someone doesn't reply within 30 minutes, the chance of the conversation continuing **drops by 60%.** Your witty message about their dog has a half-life shorter than a Snapchat story.

So no. You don't need more matches. You need to stop murdering the conversations you already have.

## How to Keep a Conversation Going Over Text (11 Rules That Actually Work)

I'm Paw Markus, and I've been analyzing dating app data at SwipeStats for years now. Our dataset covers **7,000+ real Tinder profiles, 294 million total swipes, and 3.14 million matches** (more on that in our [Tinder statistics](/blog/tinder-statistics) deep dive). I've also been on more dating apps than I care to admit. So trust me when I say these rules work. Or don't trust me. Read the data instead. I don't care about your feelings, I care about your results.

### 1. Ask Questions That Aren't a Job Interview

"What do you do?" "Where are you from?" "Any siblings?" Congratulations, you've turned a flirty text exchange into a LinkedIn screening call.

Open-ended questions beat yes/no questions. Everyone knows this. But even open-ended questions have levels. "What did you do this weekend?" is better than "hey" but it's still lazy. You're basically asking them to entertain you while you contribute nothing.

Here's what actually gets responses, broken down by age (because a 21-year-old and a 34-year-old want different things, shocking):

- **18-23:** Personality questions. "What's the most unhinged thing you've done this year?" They want energy.
- **24-28:** Lifestyle questions. "What's your ideal Sunday?" They want compatibility signals.
- **29-34:** Personal questions like "Two truths and a lie, go." This format alone has a **+34% response rate** because it's interactive and slightly competitive.
- **35+:** Pop culture references. "I see you like The Bear. Who's the Carmy in your friend group?" They want proof you're culturally literate.

The point isn't to memorize age brackets. The point is to ask questions that are actually fun to answer.

### 2. Stop Interrogating, Start Sharing

The second biggest conversation killer (right behind "hey") is the interview trap. That's when you fire off question after question without ever revealing anything about yourself. It feels exhausting to the person on the other end. Nobody wants to feel like they're being deposed by a lawyer who also finds them attractive.

Blaine Anderson calls this the "swap stories" format, and it's dead simple: ask a question, share something about yourself, then ask another question. Like a real human conversation. Revolutionary concept.

"What's the best trip you've taken recently? I just got back from Lisbon and I'm still recovering from the pasteis de nata addiction."

See how that works? You asked. You shared. You gave them something to respond to. Two hooks instead of one.

And if you're the only one asking questions? That's not a technique problem. That's a signal. They're not interested. No amount of clever follow-ups will fix someone who doesn't want to talk to you.

### 3. Actually Read Their Profile (It's Right There, You Walnut)

This one is so obvious it physically hurts me to type it. But here we are. Referencing something from their profile raises your response likelihood by **50%.** Half. Five-zero percent. And yet most of you open with "hey what's up" like their entire personality isn't sitting right there in their bio and prompts.

High-response phrases that prove you actually looked:

- **"you mention..."** (41% reply rate)
- **"curious what..."** (43% reply rate)
- **"noticed that..."** (42% reply rate)
- **"good taste..."** (40% reply rate)

All of those crush the average. It's almost like people respond better when they feel seen as an individual rather than being mass-messaged by someone with the conversational range of a fortune cookie.

Need help with specific openers? We've got a whole post on [conversation starters](/blog/best-conversation-starters-over-text) that people actually reply to.

### 4. Find Their Golden Nugget

Courtney Ryan has this technique she picked up from (of all places) sorority recruitment. It's called finding someone's "golden nugget." The one topic that makes their eyes light up. The thing they could talk about for three hours without taking a breath.

Maybe it's their obscure hobby. Maybe it's their dog (it's always the dog). Maybe it's the fact that they're training for a half marathon or that they just moved to a new city.

When you find it, dig in. Ask follow-up questions about THAT thing. Don't pivot to a new topic every two messages like you're speed-dating via text. Depth beats breadth every single time. When someone gets to talk about the thing they love most, they associate that feeling with talking to you. That's psychology, not manipulation. (Okay, it's a little bit manipulation. But the good kind.)

### 5. Your Texting Has an Energy Bar. Stop Draining It.

Matthew Hussey talks about this concept and it's one of the most useful mental models for texting I've ever heard. Every communication medium has its own energy bar. Texting has one. Phone calls have a separate one. In-person conversation has another.

If you text someone all day every day, you will drain the texting energy bar no matter how funny or charming you are. The conversation starts to feel like an obligation rather than a thrill. The butterflies die. The replies get shorter. You panic and text more. They reply less. The cycle of conversational death begins.

The fix? Switch mediums. Send a voice note. Share a meme. React to their story with a photo. **84% of people say they enjoy receiving memes** (actual poll data, not something I made up). A well-timed meme does more conversational heavy lifting than five paragraphs of "so tell me about yourself."

Things to talk about over text are infinite when you stop treating texting like the only channel.

### 6. Every Message Needs a Hook (Not Just a Greeting)

"Hey how was your day" is a conversational dead end disguised as a question. The person on the other end has to do all the work of generating something interesting from nothing. Most people (reasonably) won't bother.

Lead with something specific. A story. A hot take. A photo of something weird you saw on the way to work. "I just witnessed a man walking three cats on leashes and I need to process this with someone" is infinitely better than "wyd."

The data backs this up. Optimal first message length is **40-90 characters** (roughly 1-2 sentences). Long enough to give them something to work with. Short enough that it doesn't look like you're writing a cover letter.

And choose your topics wisely. Research shows that conversations about travel lead to **18% wanting a second date** vs only 9% for conversations about movies. So maybe stop asking "seen anything good on Netflix lately?" and start asking about their last trip or their dream destination.

### 7. Build Inside Jokes Early

Here's a conversation starter over text that never fails: callback humor. Reference something from earlier in your conversation and make it a bit. Give them a nickname based on something they said. Create a running gag about the time they admitted they can't cook.

Inside jokes create a private world between two people. Suddenly you're not just "some person from Hinge." You're "the one who called me a pasta criminal." That's intimacy manufactured through humor, and it's incredibly effective.

You never need to scramble for new topics when you have inside jokes. They're renewable energy for conversations. Every time you reference one, it reinforces the bond and gives the other person a little dopamine hit of "this person remembers what I said and found it worth remembering."

### 8. Double Text. Your Ego Will Survive.

I know. I know. Every fiber of your being screams "DON'T DO IT. THEY'LL THINK YOU'RE DESPERATE." But the data doesn't care about your fragile ego.

Hinge studied over **300,000 conversations** and found that a follow-up message gets a response **1 in 3 times.** If you don't follow up? **1 in 500.** That's a 166x improvement in response rate for the low cost of slightly bruised pride.

People get busy. Messages get buried. Notifications get swiped away. A second text that says "okay I'm taking your silence as agreement that pineapple belongs on pizza" is charming, not desperate.

We wrote an entire piece on [the art of double texting](/blog/double-texting) if you want the full breakdown. But the short version is: send the damn text.

### 9. Know When to Stop Texting and Ask Them Out

Here's the stat that should change how you think about texting forever: **the average phone number exchange happens at message 27.** Successful conversations that lead to dates average around **30 messages.** Unsuccessful ones? About 11.

Notice what that means. The conversations that go somewhere don't go on forever via text. They escalate. They move from the app to a phone number to an actual plan to meet in person.

Matthew Hussey calls this "gracious impatience." It's the energy of "I like talking to you AND I want to actually see you in real life." Something like: "Okay this is fun but I'm way funnier in person. Drinks Thursday?"

**49% of young women and 47% of men expect replies within 15 minutes.** That level of investment deserves to become a real-life interaction. The goal of learning how to keep a conversation going over text is NOT to text forever. It's to text well enough that you earn the date.

### 10. Don't Overthink Your Reply Speed

While we're on timing. **75% of people worry about replying too fast.** They think it makes them look desperate. They play the "wait exactly 47 minutes so I look busy" game. This is stupid.

**52% of people actually view quick responses positively.** They interpret it as enthusiasm and interest, not desperation. The ones who think fast replies are "too eager" are the same people who think you need to "play hard to get" and honestly they're exhausting to date anyway.

Reply when you see the message. Reply when you have something good to say. Don't engineer fake delays based on anxiety-driven game theory you learned from a Reddit thread in 2019.

### 11. Compliment Without Being a Creep

This is the tightrope. You want to show interest. You want to make them feel good. You also want to not end up screenshotted in a group chat with the caption "look at this weirdo."

The rule is simple: compliment choices, not bodies. "Your travel photos are incredible, where was that sunset shot?" works. "You're so hot" on the third message does not. Compliment their taste, their humor, their energy. Not their physical features. At least not until you've built enough rapport that it feels natural rather than predatory.

## How to Not Be Dry Over Text (A Self-Diagnosis)

Let's play a game. Read these patterns and see if you recognize yourself. Be honest. Nobody's watching. (Except me. I'm always watching.)

**The "haha nice" person:** Someone shares an entire story about their weekend and you respond with two words. You think you're being chill. You're being a brick wall. Fix: match their energy. If they wrote three sentences, write at least two back.

**The one-word responder:** "Cool." "Yeah." "Lol." "Sure." You're not mysterious. You're boring. And the other person is already composing their unmatch. Fix: if your reply is under five words, you're not contributing to a conversation. You're sending a read receipt with extra steps.

**The 11pm "wyd" texter:** We both know what you're doing. And so do they. If the only time you reach out is late at night with the creativity of a dial tone, you're not building a connection. You're sending a booty call in Morse code. Fix: text during daylight hours. About something specific. Like a human who has thoughts before 11pm.

**The question machine:** You fire off question after question and never share anything about yourself. It feels like a census, not a conversation. Fix: for every question you ask, share something about yourself. We covered this in rule 2 but some of you need to hear things twice.

## Questions to Keep a Conversation Going Over Text

Fine. You want to know exactly what to say to keep a conversation going over text. I get it. Sometimes the brain just goes blank and you need a cheat sheet. Here are some that actually work, organized by where you are in the conversation.

### Just Matched (Messages 1-5)

- "I have a very important question. Tabs or spaces?" (Works on anyone remotely nerdy. Which is everyone on dating apps.)
- "Your [specific thing from profile] caught my eye. What's the story there?"
- "Two truths and a lie. I'll go first: I've been skydiving, I can juggle, and I once ate an entire pizza in one sitting." (The pizza one is always true.)
- "What's the most unhinged impulse purchase you've ever made?"

### Been Chatting a Few Days (Messages 6-15)

- "Okay real talk. What's your controversial food opinion? Mine is that cereal is soup."
- "If you could only eat one cuisine for the rest of your life, what are you picking and why is it wrong?"
- "What's something you're weirdly competitive about?"
- "I need your expert opinion on something completely unimportant." (Then send the actual question as a follow-up. Built-in double text.)

### Conversation Going Cold (The Rescue Attempts)

- "I just saw [something that relates to earlier conversation topic] and thought of you"
- Send a meme related to something they mentioned. No caption needed.
- "Okay I've been meaning to ask. [Callback to something from earlier in the conversation]?"
- "So are we going to keep texting forever or can I take you somewhere that serves actual food?"

That last one is what Hussey calls "gracious impatience." It's confident. It's direct. And it forces a decision, which is exactly what a dying conversation needs.

## When to Let a Conversation Die (Yes, Sometimes That's the Move)

Not every conversation deserves CPR. Sometimes the kindest thing you can do (for yourself) is recognize the signs and walk away with your dignity intact.

**They haven't asked you a single question in 3+ messages.** You're performing a monologue. They're the audience, and they're checking their phone during your show. That's not conversation. That's content creation for an audience of one who isn't paying attention.

**One-word replies have become the pattern, not the exception.** Everyone sends a "haha" or "nice" occasionally. But if that's ALL you're getting? They've checked out. They just haven't bothered to unmatch yet because it requires effort and they can't even muster the energy to type full sentences.

**24+ hour gaps between responses (consistently).** There's a difference between "busy" and "not interested." Busy people still find time to text people they're excited about. Not interested people "forget" to reply for a day and a half and then send "sorry been so busy!!" with exactly that many exclamation marks.

The difference between these two states is obvious if you're being honest with yourself. The hard part isn't figuring it out. The hard part is accepting it.

Walk away. There are literally millions of people on these apps. Our SwipeStats data shows the average male right-swipe rate is **53%**. That means more than half the profiles you see are getting a shot. Your next great conversation is a few swipes away. Stop trying to resuscitate dead ones.
