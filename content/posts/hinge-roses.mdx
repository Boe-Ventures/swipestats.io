## What Are Hinge Roses? (The Bachelor Meets Your Empty Inbox)

Hinge roses are a premium like feature that lets you signal "I'm actually interested in you, not just mindlessly swiping while waiting for my burrito." They launched in December 2020, inspired by (you guessed it) The Bachelor. Because nothing says "modern romance" like borrowing mechanics from a show where 25 people compete for one person's attention on national television.

Here's how a rose differs from a regular like: when you send someone a regular like, your profile gets tossed into their Likes You pile alongside every other person who tapped the heart button that day. A rose puts you at the **top** of that pile. It's the difference between shouting into a crowd and walking up to someone with a megaphone.

Tim MacGougan, Hinge's Chief Product Officer, designed the scarcity intentionally to keep roses "sacred." Translation: they want each rose to feel meaningful so you'll pay real money when one per week doesn't cut it. Marketing genius or emotional manipulation? Both. Definitely both.

If you're familiar with other dating apps, roses are Hinge's version of Tinder's [Super Like](/blog/super-like-on-tinder) or Bumble's [SuperSwipe](/blog/bumble-superswipe). Same concept, different branding. Hinge just went with the romantic angle instead of slapping "Super" in front of the word "Like" and calling it a day.

## How Do Hinge Roses Actually Work? (The Mechanics Nobody Explains Well)

Let's break this down so simply that even someone who still uses "hey" as an opener can follow along.

**Every user gets 1 free rose per week.** Doesn't matter if you're on Free, Hinge+, or HingeX. Everyone gets one. This is Hinge's version of equality, and it's about the only thing that's equal about the experience.

Your free rose [resets every Sunday](/blog/when-do-hinge-roses-reset). Not Monday. Not "7 days from when you used it." Sunday. And there's no rollover. If you forgot to use last week's rose because you were busy being "not that desperate" (sure, buddy), it's gone forever.

You can send roses from two places in the app:

- **Discover feed:** Where you do your normal swiping. Any profile here can receive a rose instead of a regular like.
- **Standouts feed:** The curated feed of popular profiles. Roses are the **only** way to interact with these people. More on this money trap later.

Here's a tip that'll save you from looking like you don't read instructions: **add your comment BEFORE tapping the rose icon.** The rose sends immediately when you tap it. There's no "add a message" screen after. If you tap the rose first, congratulations. You just sent a silent rose, which is basically a more expensive version of the "hey" you were already sending.

What the recipient sees: your profile appears at the top of their Likes You feed with a little rose indicator. That's it. No fireworks. No singing telegram. Just your profile, wearing a tiny floral accessory, hoping someone notices.

## How Much Do Hinge Roses Cost? (Spoiler: More Than Your Self-Respect)

Let's talk money. Your free rose is, well, free. But Hinge knows that one rose per week is like giving someone one chip from a bag of Doritos. You're going to want more.

Here's the pricing breakdown:

| Bundle | Price | Per Rose |
|--------|-------|----------|
| 1 rose/week | Free | $0 |
| 3 roses | ~$9.99 | ~$3.33 |
| 12 roses | ~$29.99 | ~$2.50 |
| 50 roses | ~$49.99+ | ~$1.00-$1.87 |

The good news: **purchased roses never expire**. So if you buy 50 and only use 3 before deleting the app in a fit of existential dread, the remaining 47 will be waiting for you when you inevitably come crawling back.

The bad news: **neither Hinge+ ($32.99/mo) nor HingeX ($49.99/mo) include extra roses.** That's right. You can drop fifty bucks a month on a [Hinge subscription](/blog/hinge-subscription) and still only get one measly rose per week. Roses are a completely separate purchase. Match Group (Hinge's parent company) really said "why have one revenue stream when you can have two?"

Prices also vary by region and whether you're on iOS or Android. Apple's 30% cut means iPhone users typically pay more. Just another reason your Android-using friend is smugly superior about something.

## Standouts and Roses: Hinge's Most Genius Money Trap

This is where it gets spicy. Let me introduce you to Standouts, the feature that exists to separate you from your money as efficiently as possible.

Every day, Hinge shows you about **10 curated "Standout" profiles.** These are the app's most popular users. The people getting the most likes. The ones whose photos look like they were shot by a professional (because they probably were). The profiles you actually want to match with.

And here's the kicker: **roses are the only way to interact with Standout profiles.** You can't send a regular like. You can't send a comment. Rose or nothing.

Let's do the math together, because I know math isn't why you downloaded a dating app, but stay with me. Hinge shows you roughly **70 Standout profiles per week.** You get **1 free rose per week.** That's a 1.4% interaction rate with the profiles Hinge is actively dangling in front of your face. The other 98.6% require your credit card.

Justin McLeod, Hinge's CEO, has described Standouts as "people that are a bit more weighted towards, you're likely to like them versus they're likely to like you back." Read that again. He's telling you, politely, that these are people out of your league who Hinge is showing you anyway because they know you'll pay for roses. That's not a dating feature. That's a slot machine with better lighting.

If you want to understand how Hinge decides who shows up as a Standout, check out our [Hinge algorithm breakdown](/blog/hinge-algorithm). Spoiler: it's not random.

## Do Hinge Roses Actually Work? (Let's Be Honest)

Hinge claims that roses are **2x more likely to lead to a date** compared to regular likes. They also say that adding a comment to your rose gives you a **3x response rate.** Those are impressive numbers. Let me ruin them for you.

The "2x more likely" stat comes from internal beta testing. Hinge never specified what the baseline was. 2x more likely than what? If regular likes lead to a date 0.5% of the time, roses leading to dates 1% of the time is technically "2x" but still basically nothing. The stat is technically true and practically meaningless without context.

The **3x response rate with a comment** is more useful, and honestly, it tracks. A rose with a thoughtful comment says "I specifically chose you and have something to say." A rose without a comment says "I have more money than conversation skills."

But here's the uncomfortable truth that no amount of roses can fix: **roses amplify your profile. They don't improve it.** If your photos are mid, your prompts are boring, and your bio reads like a LinkedIn summary, a rose just spotlights all of that harder.

Reddit is full of people who learned this the hard way. "I haven't matched with a single rose, honestly" is a sentiment you'll find repeated across every dating app subreddit. YouTube creator Ali Jackson has pointed out that Standout recipients are the most popular users on the platform, already flooded with attention. Your rose is competing with dozens of other roses from people who also thought they were special.

A rose doesn't make you more attractive. It just makes sure someone actually sees you. If what they see isn't compelling, that rose was a $3.33 donation to Match Group's quarterly earnings.

## Rose Jail: The Thing Hinge Pretends Doesn't Exist

If you've spent any time on dating app TikTok (and let's be honest, you have), you've probably heard the term "Rose Jail." The hashtag #rosejail has racked up roughly **1 million views** on TikTok, which tells you this isn't some fringe conspiracy.

Rose Jail describes the experience where the most attractive profiles you encounter all seem to be in Standouts, requiring roses to interact with. Your Discover feed, the one where you can send free likes, starts feeling like the clearance rack at TJ Maxx while all the good stuff is behind a velvet rope.

Justin McLeod addressed this directly in a Fortune interview in January 2024, saying "we don't really have an attractiveness score." Which is the dating app equivalent of a restaurant saying "we don't really have a VIP section" while you watch people getting escorted past you to the nice tables.

Some people have tried to hack their way out. The most viral attempt came from Mamamia, where a user deleted their profile, recreated it on a Sunday at noon, and rejected everyone to try gaming the algorithm. The result: 429 likes, almost no compatible matches, and the kind of emotional exhaustion you'd normally need a bad relationship to achieve. It didn't work.

Here's my honest take: Rose Jail isn't a conspiracy. It's supply and demand. Popular people get sorted into Standouts because they're popular. That's it. Hinge isn't punishing you. The algorithm is just very efficiently showing you what you want and then charging you to interact with it. Is that exploitative? Maybe. Is it effective business? Absolutely. Is there a difference? Ask Match Group's shareholders.

## When to Send a Rose (And When You're Wasting Everyone's Time)

You've got one free rose per week. Treat it like the last beer in the fridge. Don't waste it on something you're lukewarm about.

**The "hell yes" test:** If a profile doesn't make you think "hell yes, I want to talk to this person," save the rose. A "they seem nice, I guess" is not rose-worthy. That's what regular likes are for.

**ALWAYS include a comment.** I cannot stress this enough. The 3x response rate increase from adding a comment is one of the few Hinge statistics that actually matters. Your comment should reference something specific in their profile. "Nice smile!" is not specific. "Your prompt about pineapple on pizza is wrong but I respect your confidence" is specific. If you need help, read our guide on [Hinge openers](/blog/best-hinge-openers).

**The Saturday night rule:** If it's Saturday and you still haven't used your free rose, open the app and send it to the best option available. A used rose on a "pretty good" profile beats a wasted rose every time. Letting it expire is like leaving money on the table, except the money is shaped like a flower and represents your dwindling romantic prospects.

**When NOT to send a rose:**

- Profiles with one photo and no prompts (they're not trying, so why should you?)
- Someone with zero shared interests (the rose won't bridge that gap)
- When your comment would be generic. If you can't think of something specific to say, the rose is doing all the heavy lifting, and roses don't have great upper body strength
- When your own profile isn't ready. Fix your [photos and bio](/blog/best-hinge-profiles-for-guys) before buying roses. A rose on a weak profile is like putting a spotlight on a crime scene

## Hinge Roses vs. Super Like vs. SuperSwipe (The Premium Like Olympics)

Every major dating app has its own version of "I like you MORE than a regular like." Here's how they stack up:

| Feature | Hinge Rose | Tinder Super Like | Bumble SuperSwipe |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Free allowance | 1/week | Varies by tier | 1/week |
| Cost per unit | ~$3.33 | Part of subscription | ~$3-5 |
| What recipient sees | Top of Likes You + rose icon | Blue border notification | Yellow highlight |
| Gate-kept content | Standouts (rose-only) | No | No |
| Notification | Yes | Yes | Yes |

The biggest difference: Hinge is the only app that locks an entire section of profiles behind the premium interaction. Tinder doesn't hide attractive people in a separate feed that requires Super Likes. Bumble doesn't make you pay a premium swipe to see popular users. Only Hinge decided to create a premium showcase and then charge admission.

Whether that's genius product design or predatory monetization depends on which side of the transaction you're on.

## FAQ (Because You're Still Here and Somehow Still Confused)

### When do Hinge roses reset?

Every **Sunday**. One free rose, no rollover, no exceptions. For the full breakdown on timing and strategy, check out [when Hinge roses reset](/blog/when-do-hinge-roses-reset).

### How many roses do you get on Hinge?

**1 free rose per week.** Every user, every subscription tier. Buy more if you want, but Hinge isn't handing them out like candy.

### Do purchased roses expire?

**No.** Purchased roses stay in your account until you use them. The only rose that expires is your weekly freebie.

### Is sending a rose on Hinge weird or cringe?

Not at all. It's a built-in feature that everyone knows about. What IS cringe is sending a rose with no comment attached. That's like handing someone flowers and then staring at them in silence. Say something. Anything. Preferably something that shows you actually looked at their profile.

### Do Hinge+ or HingeX give more free roses?

**No.** This is the most common misconception about [Hinge's paid tiers](/blog/is-hinge-plus-worth-it). Both paid tiers give you the same single free rose per week as free users. Roses are sold separately because Match Group likes money.

### How often do you get roses on Hinge?

**1 per week free**, resetting Sunday. You can buy more anytime from the app. The frequency of receiving roses depends entirely on how many people think your profile is worth a $3.33 investment. Take that as motivation to improve your profile.

### What are roses on Hinge?

Premium likes with priority placement. They push your profile to the top of someone's Likes You feed and are the only way to interact with Standout profiles. Think of them as Hinge's way of saying "put your money where your swipe is."
