## Why Your Texts Are Getting Ignored (It's Not Your Face)

Let's get one thing out of the way: if a guy isn't responding to your texts, it's almost certainly not because you're unattractive. He already matched with you. He already saw your photos. He swiped right. The attraction part is handled.

The problem is that your messages are the conversational equivalent of elevator music. Present, technically audible, and completely ignorable.

**84% of "hey" openers get ignored.** That's not a typo. That's [real research from Bruch and Newman](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.aap9815) analyzing millions of messages on dating platforms. And yet every single day, millions of people (yes, women too) type those three letters, hit send, and then stare at their phone wondering why God has forsaken them.

Here's the thing that should make you feel either very empowered or very embarrassed: when women send first messages, they're **25% more likely to get a response** than men's first messages. You have a built-in statistical advantage. Men need to send **58 messages** to get a 90% chance of getting just one reply. Women need **13**. Thirteen. You could send one message a day for two weeks and statistically guarantee yourself a conversation.

And you're still losing. Which means the problem isn't volume. The problem is that you're sending texts that require zero effort to compose and zero effort to ignore. Your opener reads like a form letter, and he's treating it like one.

From our SwipeStats analysis of 7,000+ real Tinder profiles and 294M swipes, we can see that **43.4% of matches result in zero to one messages exchanged.** That's nearly half of all matches dying in the text thread before they even draw a breath. The bar is underground. You just have to step over it.

## The 40-90 Character Sweet Spot (Novels Don't Get Replies Either)

So you know "hey" doesn't work. But what does? A novel about your childhood? Your entire personality crammed into one paragraph? A list of your top five movies?

No. None of that.

Analysis of over one million conversations shows that the optimal first message lands between **40 and 90 characters**. That's one to two sentences. Enough to say something real. Short enough that it doesn't feel like homework.

The technique that obliterates everything else is the photo-reference opener. Something like: "Your [specific item] in photo 3 is incredible. Where's it from?" That's it. That generated a **73% reply rate** compared to a 21% baseline. That's a 340% improvement from doing one simple thing: proving you actually looked at his profile.

Why does this work? Three reasons. First, cognitive ease. It's easy to process a short, specific question. His brain doesn't have to do any work. Second, low commitment. You're not asking him to bare his soul. You're asking about a jacket. Third, implicit reciprocity. When someone shows genuine interest in you, social psychology says you feel a pull to return it. It's the same reason you feel weird not holding the door for someone who held it for you.

Now here's the plot twist that'll annoy every dating advice blog on the internet: generic "ask a question" openers actually perform **15% worse** than average. "Be original" openers? Down 23%. Humor-focused openers? **68% failure rate.** You read that right. Trying to be funny in your first message backfires most of the time. So put down the [pickup lines](/blog/best-rizz-pickup-lines) for now and try being observant instead.

One more stat that should change how you text forever: first messages containing the phrase "you mention" raise response likelihood by **50%**. Referencing something from his bio or prompts isn't just nice. It's the single most effective thing you can do. It signals "I'm not mass-messaging," which, in a world where most people are doing exactly that, makes you rare.

## How to Flirt With a Guy Through Texting (Without the Cringe)

Alright. You've sent the first message. He replied. Now you're sitting there trying to figure out how to flirt with a guy through texting without sounding like a Hallmark card written by a chatbot.

Here's your playbook.

**The assumptive invite.** Instead of "Do you want to get coffee sometime?" try "I'm checking out that new place on 5th this Saturday. You should come." Notice the difference? No question mark. No option to say "maybe." You're not asking permission. You're extending a confident invitation and assuming he's smart enough to accept. This works because decisiveness is attractive. Nobody fantasizes about someone who says "I don't know, what do YOU want to do?"

**Compliment his choices, not his genetics.** This one comes from Matthew Hussey and it's brilliant. "Those shoes are fire" beats "You have nice eyes" every time. Why? He didn't choose his eyes. He chose the shoes. When you compliment a decision, you're telling him he has good taste. When you compliment his face, you're telling him he won the genetic lottery, which he already knew (or didn't, and now it's weird).

**The "adorably demanding" technique.** Ready for this one? "Are you actually going to ask me out, or can I just expect a 'How's your week?' for the rest of my life?" followed by a wink emoji. The wink is critical. It says "I have standards AND I'm playful about it." Without the wink, you sound like his mother. With it, you sound like someone worth pursuing.

**Send picture messages with context.** Not selfies. Context. Saw something that reminded you of a joke he made? Snap it, send it. At the weird coffee shop he mentioned? Photo. This is how you build inside jokes before you've even had a first date. Inside jokes create intimacy. Intimacy creates attraction. I've seen this pattern play out in thousands of conversations in our SwipeStats data, and the ones that convert to actual dates (only **23% of chats** do, by the way) almost always have this back-and-forth texture to them.

## Texts That Actually Work (Backed by Data, Not Your Horoscope)

I'm going to be honest with you. Most "flirty texts to send a guy" articles read like they were written by someone who learned about human interaction from a textbook. "Tell him he's special!" Great, Karen. Ground-breaking stuff.

Here's what actually works, based on psychology research and the patterns we see in real conversations.

**The "I feel safe with you" text.** This phrase hits masculine psychology like a freight train. It's not just a compliment. It tells him that his presence has a tangible effect on you. That he's not just nice to look at, but that he provides something. Men are wired to respond to feeling needed, and "I feel safe with you" activates that without being manipulative. Just don't send it after the first date. Timing matters (more on that below).

**The recognition text.** Appreciate what he does, not what he is. "Thank you for being so patient when I was stressed about work" beats "You're so nice" by a mile. The first one acknowledges specific effort. The second one is something his grandma says. Men stay invested when they feel their effort is seen. [Research on relationship satisfaction](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8669216/) backs this up: perceived partner responsiveness is one of the strongest predictors of relationship interest.

**Compliment the trait behind the talent.** If he plays guitar, don't say "You're amazing at guitar." Everyone says that. Say "The discipline it took to get that good? That's what's attractive." You just leapfrogged every other woman who's complimented his chord progressions. You saw the person behind the skill. That's rare and he'll remember it.

**Memory texts in present tense.** "Remember when we were at that taco place and you spilled salsa everywhere?" puts him back IN the moment. It's reliving the experience, not just recalling it. Present-tense memory triggers are a trick fiction writers use to create immersion. Works just as well in a text thread. (Yes, I just compared texting to literature. I write a dating blog for a living. Let me have this.)

## When to Text Him (The Timing Data Nobody Talks About)

You can write the perfect message and still get ghosted because you sent it at 7 AM on a Monday when he's contemplating whether life has meaning while staring at his email inbox.

Timing matters more than most people realize, and the data is surprisingly clear.

**Best send times:** Sunday 6-8 PM gets a **43% reply lift.** Tuesday 7-9 PM gives you **31% more responses.** Thursday 8-10 PM nets **28% more.** These are the windows when people are relaxed, done with obligations, and actually looking at their phones for fun instead of duty.

**Worst times:** Friday nights (he's either out or wants to look like he is), Monday mornings (existential dread), and after 11 PM (you'll either look drunk or desperate, possibly both).

**The 30-minute cliff.** If you don't get a reply within 30 minutes, the chance of the conversation continuing drops by **60%.** That doesn't mean you should panic-text at minute 31. It means that initial response windows matter, and sending messages when he's likely available dramatically increases your odds.

**After a first date: text the next morning.** [A study of 543 people](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/02654075251377184) found that next-morning texters scored **6.15 out of 9** on relationship interest, compared to 5.50 for people who waited two days. Playing hard to get doesn't make you look mysterious. It makes you look like you're not interested. Or worse, like you're playing games. And no adult with a functioning life wants to date someone who treats communication like a chess match.

One more thing: women are more sensitive to response timing than men. He might genuinely not care whether you texted at 2 PM or 9 PM. But your brain will absolutely spiral if he takes six hours to reply on a Tuesday. Knowing this about yourself is half the battle.

## How to Keep a Guy Interested Over Text (Without Becoming His Therapist)

Congratulations, you've started a conversation that didn't die after three messages. Now comes the part where most people blow it: the middle.

The beginning gets all the advice. The [first message](/blog/first-message-on-dating-app) is what everyone obsesses over. But conversations don't die because of bad openers. They die because they flatline in the middle, turning into a polite exchange of "haha yeah" and "that's crazy" until someone quietly stops replying and neither of you acknowledges it, like two coworkers who both saw each other at the grocery store and chose not to make eye contact.

Here's how to keep a guy interested while texting.

**Quality over quantity.** One well-crafted message beats twelve "hahaha"s. If you're sending more messages than he is, slow down. Match his energy. If he's sending full sentences, send full sentences. If he's sending three words, either escalate the topic or let the conversation breathe. What you should not do is compensate for his low effort with your high effort. That's a recipe for resentment.

**Mix your media.** Voice memos. Photos with context. Even (gasp) phone calls. Texting-only relationships create artificial intimacy that collapses on the first real date when you realize you have no idea what their voice sounds like or how they react to things in real time. I've seen so many profiles in our SwipeStats dataset where the match rate was solid but the conversion to actual dates was abysmal. The common thread? Weeks of texting with zero escalation.

**End conversations at a high point, not when they die.** This is the "leave them wanting more" principle and it works because of something called the Zeigarnik effect. Our brains fixate on unfinished experiences. If you stop texting when things are funny and interesting, he'll think about the conversation later. If you text until the conversation degrades into "lol" and "same," he'll remember it as boring. Leave before the party's over. George Costanza was right about this one.

**The transition is everything.** Texting is a bridge to real life, not a destination. If you're four days into a text conversation and haven't made plans to meet, you're not building a relationship. You're pen pals. Move things offline before you've said everything interesting about yourself. For [keeping conversation going](/blog/how-to-keep-a-conversation-going-over-text), you need forward momentum, not just maintenance.

**Long-distance exception:** if you're actually in a long-distance situation, texting frequency genuinely predicts relationship satisfaction. For same-city dating? It doesn't. Just call. Or better yet, walk to the same coffee shop and sit across from each other like humans have done for centuries.

## What NOT to Text Him (The Blacklist)

I've been writing this blog long enough to know that some of you skipped straight to this section. Fine. Here's the blacklist.

**"Hey" / "What's up?" / "Hi"** The conversational equivalent of a limp handshake. You didn't just read 2,000 words about how to [start a conversation](/blog/how-to-start-a-conversation-on-hinge) only to send "hey." I will find out. I will be disappointed.

**Physical appearance compliments early on.** Words like "hot," "sexy," and "beautiful" in early messages correlate negatively with response rates according to the [Pew Research data](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2023/02/02/the-experiences-of-u-s-online-daters/). I know this sounds backwards. But telling a guy he's hot in message two makes the whole thing feel transactional. Save it for when you've built some rapport.

**Drunk texts and emotional dumps.** Vulnerable conversations belong in person, where tone of voice and body language prevent misinterpretation. Over text, your "I've been thinking about what we are" reads like a hostage situation. Wait for the date.

**Double-texting marathons.** If he hasn't replied, sending four more messages isn't going to fix it. It's going to make his phone look like an amber alert went off. If he wanted to respond, he would. If he's busy, he'll get to it. If he's not interested, your fifth text isn't the one that changes his mind.

**The "validate me" trap.** Don't mirror low-effort texts with maximum investment. If he sends "miss you," the correct response is "Aw, that's sweet :)" and not "miss you too!!! I was just thinking about you!!! When can I see you again???" Match. His. Energy. Or redirect the conversation somewhere more interesting. Your self-respect will thank you.

## FAQ

### How do you text a guy to make him like you?

You don't "make" anyone like you through text. What you can do is stop being forgettable. Reference something specific from his profile (50% more responses). Keep messages between 40-90 characters. Be direct about meeting in person. The women who do best in our SwipeStats data aren't the ones with the cleverest lines. They're the ones who are specific, confident, and actually move conversations toward real dates. If you want more opening line ideas, check out our guide on [Bumble openers](/blog/best-bumble-openers).

### How do you keep a guy interested while texting?

End conversations at their peak, not when they're dying. Mix media types (voice notes, photos with context, the occasional actual phone call). Don't become his therapist or his entertainment system. And for the love of everything, transition to a real date within the first week. Texting is a bridge. Stop building a house on it.

### How do you know if a guy likes you through text?

He responds quickly and consistently. He asks you questions (not just answers yours). He remembers details from previous conversations. He initiates conversations, not just replies to yours. And the biggest tell: he suggests meeting up. A guy who likes you over text wants to see you in person. A guy who only likes texting you is either bored, lonely, or collecting pen pals.

### What should you text a guy to start a conversation?

Reference something specific from his profile. "Your [thing] in photo 3 is amazing, where's it from?" generates **73% reply rates** compared to 21% for generic openers. Messages with "you mention" get **50% more responses.** The pattern is clear: specificity beats creativity. You don't need to be witty. You need to prove you paid attention.
