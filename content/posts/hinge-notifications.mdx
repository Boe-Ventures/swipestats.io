### TL;DR. Your Phone Isn't Broken (Probably)

Look, Hinge notifications are simple in theory and an absolute mess in practice. Here's what you need to know before you spiral:

- Hinge sends 5 types of push notifications: matches, messages, likes, Most Compatible, and Standouts. That's it. Not complicated.
- Ghost notifications (the buzz with nothing in your inbox) are a known Hinge bug, not someone unmatching you. Well, usually.
- Android users get the worst of it. Samsung and Xiaomi phones are so aggressive about killing background apps that someone literally reverse-engineered Hinge's API just to get working notifications.
- **Most Compatible matches lead to 8x more dates** according to Hinge's own data. So maybe don't ignore the one notification that actually matters.
- The fix order when things break: in-app settings > phone settings > battery optimization > uninstall and reinstall > throw phone at wall (optional).

</TLDR>

## Every Type of Hinge Notification (And What Each One Actually Means)

Hinge notifications fall into a few neat categories. If your phone ever actually delivers them. But we'll get to that dumpster fire later.

First, let's talk about what you're supposed to be getting.

### Match Notifications (The Dopamine Hit)

This is the big one. Someone liked you back, and Hinge wants to make sure you know about it before the excitement wears off and reality sets in.

When someone you've sent a like to also likes you back (or responds to your [match note](/blog/hinge-match-note)), you'll get a push notification that says something like "You have a new match!" Original, I know.

Once you're in a conversation, Hinge uses a "Your Turn" / "Their Turn" system. Blue badge means it's your turn to reply. Think of it as a polite nudge that says "Hey, you're being rude." Red badge means a new like is waiting for you to review. These aren't push notifications exactly. They're in-app badges. But they're worth knowing about because they'll stare at you every time you open the app like a disappointed parent.

### Like and Comment Notifications (Someone Noticed You Exist)

When someone sends you a like or leaves a comment on one of your prompts, Hinge pings you. This is your "someone out there finds you tolerable" notification.

There's a hierarchy here. A basic like is the equivalent of a head nod across the bar. A comment on your prompt means they actually read something you wrote (rare). And a [rose](/blog/hinge-roses)? That's the premium "I'm really into you" signal, since roses cost actual money.

Free users only get limited info about who liked them. You'll know someone liked you, but seeing the full picture requires Hinge's paid tier. Because of course it does.

### Most Compatible and Standouts (The VIP Treatment)

Most Compatible is the notification you should actually care about. Once a day, Hinge's [algorithm](/blog/hinge-algorithm) serves you a match it thinks is perfect for you based on your preferences and behavior patterns.

Here's the stat that should make you sit up: **Most Compatible matches are 8x more likely to lead to a date** according to Hinge's own data. Eight times. That's not a rounding error. That's the difference between staying home watching reruns and actually putting on real pants to meet a human being.

Standouts get a daily refresh too. About 10 profiles that Hinge considers top-tier in your area. The catch? You need roses to interact with them. Hinge isn't running a charity here.

### Email Notifications (For the People Who Still Check Email)

Hinge also sends email notifications, which is adorable in a "grandma still uses AOL" kind of way. These are separate from push notifications and handle the less urgent stuff: weekly activity digests, account security updates, promotional offers.

You can toggle these independently from push notifications. Most people turn them off and forget they exist, which is honestly the right call.

## What Do Hinge Push Notifications Look Like on Your Phone?

On your lock screen, Hinge notifications show up with the app's little "H" icon and a brief preview. "You have a new match!" or "Someone sent you a comment." Nothing groundbreaking. No profile picture preview, no sneak peek at the message. Just enough to get you to open the app, which is the whole point.

iOS and Android handle the display slightly differently. iOS users get the notification grouped neatly in their notification center. Android users get... well, Android users get whatever their phone manufacturer decided to do that day (more on this nightmare later).

Inside the app, Hinge organizes notifications into four categories: **New Likes, New Matches, New Messages, and Promotions**. You can control each one independently, which is nice. The in-app badges work like this: red badge on the Likes tab means someone new has liked your profile. Blue badge in your conversations means it's your turn to respond. If you're seeing a blue badge and ignoring it, just know that conversation is slowly dying. After 14 days of silence, Hinge moves the chat to "Hidden" with zero notification. Poof. Gone. Like it never happened.

As of 2025, Hinge also added chat-specific notification preferences. You can choose to only get alerts for the conversations you're actually excited about. Which is Hinge's polite way of saying "we know you're juggling five conversations and only care about one of them."

One thing Hinge does NOT have: read receipts, online status indicators, or quick reply from the notification. You can't tell if someone read your message and chose to ignore you. Whether that's a feature or a bug depends on how anxious you are as a person.

## Hinge Notifications Not Working? Here's the Actual Fix

This is why most of you are here. Your phone buzzes, you scramble to check it like Pavlov's thirstiest dog, and there's... nothing. Or worse, you find out three hours later that someone messaged you because you happened to open the app out of boredom. Let's fix this.

### The Ghost Notification Bug (It's Not You, It's Probably Hinge)

Ghost notifications are Hinge's signature move. You get a push notification, you open the app, and there's absolutely nothing new. No match. No message. No like. Just you, staring at your phone, questioning your sanity.

Here's what actually happens: Hinge's server sends the push notification before fully confirming the event is still valid. It's a race condition. By the time your phone receives the alert, the person may have unmatched, the like may have been retracted, or the server just hiccupped. The notification was technically accurate for about 0.3 seconds. Congratulations.

The most common causes:

- Someone unmatched you right after the notification fired
- Server sync delay between Hinge's systems
- An expired or removed match
- Hinge just being Hinge

**The fix:** Force close the app and reopen it. Pull to refresh your inbox. If that doesn't work, log out and log back in. If you're still seeing ghosts, the match is genuinely gone. If you suspect something fishier is going on, check out our guide on [matches disappearing](/blog/hinge-match-disappeared).

### Fix Hinge Notifications on iPhone

If you're not getting Hinge notifications on iPhone, run through this checklist in order. Don't skip steps. I know you want to.

1. **In-app settings first.** Open Hinge > Settings > Notifications. Make sure the categories you care about (Matches, Messages, Likes) are toggled on.
2. **iOS Settings.** Go to Settings > Notifications > Hinge. Make sure "Allow Notifications" is on. Check that your preferred alert style (banners, sounds, badges) is selected.
3. **Check Focus Mode.** This is the sneaky one. If you're using Focus Mode (Do Not Disturb, Work, Sleep), Hinge might be silenced. Go to Settings > Focus and make sure Hinge is in your allowed apps list, or just turn off Focus Mode when you're actively looking to be bothered.
4. **Notification Summary.** iOS can bundle your notifications into a scheduled digest instead of delivering them immediately. If Hinge is in your Scheduled Summary, it won't buzz you in real time. Go to Settings > Notifications > Scheduled Summary and remove Hinge from the list.
5. **Screen Time restrictions.** If you (or your overly protective past self) set Screen Time limits on Hinge, notifications may get blocked. Check Settings > Screen Time > App Limits.
6. **Nuclear option.** Offload the app (Settings > General > iPhone Storage > Hinge > Offload App) and reinstall. This clears cached data without losing your account. If that fails, full delete and reinstall.

### Fix Hinge Notifications on Android (Good Luck)

I'm going to level with you. Android notification issues with Hinge are a special kind of hell. It's not your fault. It's your phone manufacturer's fault.

Android's aggressive battery optimization silently kills Hinge's background processes. The phone decides "hey, you haven't opened this app in 20 minutes, it must not be important" and murders it. No background process means no push notifications. **Samsung, Xiaomi, and OnePlus are the worst offenders.**

How bad is it? Bad enough that a developer created an open-source project called "squeaky-hinge" on GitHub that literally reverse-engineers Hinge's private API to send email and SMS notifications instead. Because the official push notifications fail that often. Let that sink in. Someone was so frustrated with not getting Hinge notifications on Android that they built an entire alternative notification system from scratch.

Here's the fix attempt:

1. **In-app settings.** Same as iPhone. Open Hinge > Settings > Notifications > toggle everything on.
2. **Android system settings.** Go to Settings > Apps > Hinge > Notifications. Make sure notifications are enabled for all categories.
3. **Battery optimization.** This is the big one. Go to Settings > Apps > Hinge > Battery > Set to "Unrestricted" or "No optimization." On Samsung, also check Settings > Device Care > Battery > Background usage limits and make sure Hinge isn't in the "Sleeping apps" or "Deep sleeping apps" list. On Xiaomi, go to Settings > Apps > Manage Apps > Hinge > Autostart and enable it.
4. **Data Saver.** Make sure Hinge has unrestricted data access. Settings > Network > Data Saver > Unrestricted data > toggle Hinge on.
5. **Nuclear option.** Clear cache, clear data, uninstall, reinstall. If you're still not getting notifications after all of this, you have my sympathies. Consider switching to iPhone or just checking the app manually like it's 2008 and you're refreshing your MySpace page.

If nothing works, just [refresh Hinge manually](/blog/how-to-refresh-hinge) a few times a day. Not elegant, but it beats missing matches.

## Your Brain on Hinge Notifications (The Slot Machine in Your Pocket)

Let's talk about why you just checked your phone mid-sentence while reading this article.

Hinge notifications exploit the same psychological mechanism as slot machines. It's called a variable-ratio reinforcement schedule. You don't know when the reward is coming, which makes you check compulsively. A notification might be a hot match. Or it might be a ghost notification about nothing. Or it might be Hinge asking you to upgrade. The uncertainty is the point.

Cheng Chen, a former dating app designer, put it perfectly: **"Push notifications and the random rewards they offer really get our brains excited because we can't predict when we'll get a match."** Your brain treats every notification buzz as a potential jackpot. Psychiatrist Elias Aboujaoude backs this up, noting that **"a dopamine-like reward pathway may be involved"** when you get match notifications. The same reward circuit that lights up when a gambler hits a winning combination.

And the data on what this does to your mental health? Not great. A meta-analysis found that **dating app users report significantly worse outcomes across depression, anxiety, loneliness, and psychological distress** compared to non-users. Research from Tulane University found that social anxiety and loneliness actually predict compulsive dating app use. So the people most vulnerable to the dopamine trap are the ones most likely to fall into it. Fun.

Then there's the 14-day hidden chat trap I mentioned earlier. If a conversation goes quiet for two weeks, Hinge moves it to "Hidden" without any notification. No "hey, this chat is about to expire" warning. Nothing. So you get the anxiety of the notification loop AND the anxiety of conversations silently disappearing. Hinge really thought of everything.

## Should You Turn Off Hinge Notifications? (A Case for Your Sanity)

You've read this far, which means you're either genuinely curious or you're avoiding opening Hinge right now. Either way, let's talk strategy.

**The argument for turning them off:** Notification fatigue is real. If every buzz sends you into a Pavlovian spiral of hope and disappointment, that's not healthy. Turning off notifications forces you to check the app on your own schedule. You become intentional about your dating life instead of reactive to it. Some people report feeling significantly less anxious after going notification-free for even a week. Your cortisol levels will thank you.

**The argument against:** Response time matters on dating apps. Hinge likely factors in how quickly you respond. More importantly, conversations die fast. If someone messages you and you don't see it for 8 hours, they've already moved on to someone who actually showed up. And remember that 14-day auto-hide? The clock is ticking whether you know it or not.

**The middle ground (what I actually recommend):** Keep match and message notifications on. Kill everything else. No Standouts alerts. No promotional garbage. No "Check out who liked you!" nagging. This gives you the important stuff in real time while cutting out the noise that's turning your brain into soup.

Here's your notification strategy:

- **Matches:** ON. This is the whole point.
- **Messages:** ON. Don't leave people hanging.
- **New Likes:** OFF (unless you're on the paid plan and can actually act on them).
- **Promotions:** OFF. Always off. Forever off.
- **Email notifications:** OFF. You're not going to read them.
- **Most Compatible:** ON. Remember, 8x more likely to lead to a date. Don't sleep on this one.

## FAQ

### Does Hinge Notify Screenshots?

No. Hinge does not send notifications when someone screenshots your profile, your conversation, or anything else. Screenshot away, you creep. (If you're worried about other apps too, [Tinder doesn't snitch either](/blog/does-tinder-notify-screenshots).)

### Why Am I Getting Ghost Notifications on Hinge?

Ghost notifications happen because Hinge fires the push alert before confirming the match or message is still valid on the server. By the time you open the app, the person may have unmatched, the like was retracted, or the server just desynced. Force close the app, reopen, and pull to refresh. If there's genuinely nothing there, the interaction is gone. It's not a conspiracy. It's just bad engineering.

### Does Hinge Send Notifications When You're Inactive?

Yes. Hinge will send you "We miss you" and "Someone liked your profile" notifications to drag you back in, even if you haven't opened the app in weeks. This is by design. They want you back. If you want the notifications to stop without deleting your account, just disable push notifications in your phone's system settings. Pausing your profile won't fully stop them either, because Hinge is clingy like that.

### Can Someone See If You Read Their Hinge Message?

No. Hinge does not have read receipts. There's no "Seen" indicator, no blue checkmarks, no typing indicators. Your silence is invisible, which is either a relief or a torture device depending on which side of the conversation you're on.

### How Do I Turn On Hinge Notifications?

Open Hinge, tap your profile icon, go to Settings, and find the Notifications section. Toggle on the categories you want: New Likes, New Matches, New Messages, Promotions. Then make sure your phone's system settings also allow Hinge notifications (Settings > Notifications > Hinge on iOS, Settings > Apps > Hinge > Notifications on Android). If they're still not working after both steps, scroll back up to the "not working" section of this article. I put a lot of effort into that part and I'd hate for it to go to waste.
