<TLDR>

This guide is for consenting adults.

1. Ask whether sexual talk is welcome.
2. Agree on words, topics, and channels that are off-limits.
3. Start mild and let the other person actively participate.
4. Check in when intensity changes.
5. Stop immediately when asked—or when enthusiasm disappears.

</TLDR>

## Ask Before You Change the Conversation

A dating-app match, kiss, relationship, or previous sexual exchange does not create permanent consent.

Try:

- “Would you be into a more explicit conversation?”
- “Can I tell you what I have been thinking about?”
- “Do you like dirty talk, and are there words you do not want used?”
- “Are you comfortable sexting here, or would you rather keep this nonsexual?”

An enthusiastic yes gives you a starting point, not unlimited permission.

## Establish Boundaries

Before getting explicit, discuss:

- words or labels that feel good or bad;
- acts or fantasies that are off-limits;
- whether photos or voice notes are welcome;
- whether messages may be saved;
- a simple stop word or phrase;
- privacy risks on the chosen platform.

Never pressure someone to send an image. Never share intimate material without the subject's explicit permission. Laws concerning intimate images and age vary; if anyone's age is uncertain, stop.

## Start With the Present and the True

You do not need a theatrical script. Name real attraction or anticipation:

- “I keep thinking about kissing you.”
- “I liked the way you pulled me closer.”
- “I want to hear what you enjoyed most.”
- “Tell me whether you want me to keep going.”

These examples remain direct while creating a response point.

## Let It Be Collaborative

Dirty talk works better as an exchange than a monologue. Ask preferences and respond to what the other person contributes:

- “More direct or more teasing?”
- “Do you want me to describe what I want, or ask what you want?”
- “Is this still good?”
- “Slower?”

Silence, a sudden topic change, hesitant language, or reduced participation is a reason to pause and check—not to intensify.

## Use Check-Ins Without Breaking the Mood

- “Do you like that?”
- “Want more?”
- “Tell me to stop whenever you want.”
- “Is that word okay?”
- “Still with me?”

Consent can be sexy because attention is sexy.

## What to Avoid

- surprise explicit messages;
- humiliation or identity-based language not specifically agreed upon;
- threats presented as flirtation;
- describing acts the person has declined;
- screenshots or sharing with friends;
- continuing after “stop,” “not now,” discomfort, or nonparticipation;
- using AI with an identifiable private sexual conversation.

## Afterward

Check in outside the charged moment:

> I enjoyed that. Was there anything you especially liked or would rather avoid next time?

Respect a changed boundary. Consent to yesterday's conversation is not a contract for tomorrow.
