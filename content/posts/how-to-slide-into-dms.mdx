<TLDR>

### The First-DM Formula

**honest connection + public detail + answerable question**

> We met briefly at Lina's opening. You mentioned the photography show downtown—was it worth seeing?

</TLDR>

## A DM Is Access to an Inbox, Not Permission to Pursue

People use social media for different reasons. A public profile does not automatically invite romantic messages, and a follow or story view does not prove attraction. Your job is to make a respectful opening easy to understand and equally easy to decline.

## The Best Contexts

A first message has more shape when you have:

- met in person;
- a genuine mutual friend or community;
- discussed the same public topic;
- a specific response to work they intentionally shared;
- an explicit invitation to message.

Do not manufacture familiarity by studying years of posts, referencing a precise location, or mentioning private information you found elsewhere.

## First-DM Examples

### After meeting

> I enjoyed talking with you at Sam's party. You were right about that album—what should I hear next?

### Shared interest

> Your post about the neighborhood cinema convinced me to visit. Do you have a favorite screening there?

### Responding to public work

> The point in your article about beginner-friendly climbing was useful. Is there a route you would recommend for a first visit?

### Clear romantic interest

> We have only met briefly, but I enjoyed your company. If you are open to it, I would like to take you for coffee. No worries if not.

### Dating-profile link

> I saw your profile on Hinge and followed the link you chose to share. I liked your answer about independent bookstores. If you would rather keep dating messages in the app, I completely understand.

That last situation is sensitive. If the dating app does not allow contact or you did not match, moving to another platform can feel like bypassing a boundary. Usually, leave it in the app.

## Story Replies

Reply to the subject, not merely their appearance:

- “Was that trail as steep as it looks?”
- “I need the name of that restaurant.”
- “Your dog appears to have strong opinions about rain.”

A flame emoji is fast, but it gives the recipient almost nothing to answer.

## What Not to Send

- comments about their body as the first contact;
- demands for a reply;
- sexual content they did not request;
- multiple messages across several platforms;
- “I know you saw this”;
- references to their home, workplace, or routine;
- a fake business question that turns into flirting.

## Following Up

Silence is not a puzzle you must solve. If there was a real prior connection, one short follow-up after several days can be reasonable:

> Just resurfacing this once in case it got buried. No need to reply if you are not interested.

Then stop. Do not switch platforms, recruit friends, or keep reacting to stories to force visibility.

## If They Reply

Share as well as ask. If the conversation becomes reciprocal, be direct rather than indefinitely orbiting:

> I am enjoying this. Want to continue over coffee Saturday afternoon?

If they decline, thank them and leave the interaction alone.
