<TLDR>

### After the Match

Continue the detail that created the match, share something of your own, and ask one easy question. When both people are contributing, suggest a specific, low-pressure plan.

</TLDR>

## Opening Line vs. Conversation Starter

An opening line is attached to the first Like. A conversation starter is useful after the match exists—especially when the original comment has been answered and the chat needs somewhere to go.

Good conversation is reciprocal. One person should not have to supply every question, story, and follow-up.

## Continue the Original Topic

If the match began with a photo or prompt, stay there for one more turn:

> You said Lisbon won for food. What was the meal you are still thinking about?

Then contribute:

> Mine is a tiny noodle shop in Taipei that ruined airport food for the rest of the trip.

That combination—question plus disclosure—feels more like a conversation than an interview.

## Starters for a New Match

- “What is the best story behind something on your profile?”
- “Which part of your ideal weekend actually happens most often?”
- “You get to plan a two-hour date in your neighborhood. What are we doing?”
- “What opinion in your prompts causes the most debate?”
- “What are you currently learning badly but enjoying anyway?”

## Starters Based on Profile Topics

### Travel

- “Which trip changed once you arrived?”
- “What place would you revisit without making a single new plan?”

### Food

- “What dish do you use to judge a new restaurant?”
- “What is your low-effort but impressive dinner?”

### Pets

- “What personality trait did your pet train into you?”
- “Who really chose whom?”

### Work and interests

- “What part of your work is more interesting than the job title sounds?”
- “What hobby gives you the best beginner story?”

## When the Chat Goes Quiet

One follow-up can create a new opening without pretending the other person owes an answer:

- “New topic: what is one thing you are looking forward to this week?”
- “I saw the restaurant you mentioned and remembered this chat. What should I order?”
- “I enjoyed talking with you. If you are still interested, want to continue over coffee this weekend?”

If there is no reply, leave it there. Silence does not reveal why someone stopped responding, and repeated nudges rarely create the kind of connection you want.

## When to Suggest a Date

There is no evidence-backed universal message count. Look for reciprocity:

- both people answer and ask questions;
- the tone is comfortable;
- basic intentions and logistics are compatible;
- neither person is being pressured.

Then make a concrete invitation:

> I am enjoying this. Want to get coffee at North Street Sunday afternoon?

Specificity makes it easy to accept, decline, or suggest another time.

## Voice Notes and Other Chat Tools

Hinge currently supports ordinary messages, emoji, message Likes, and Voice Notes. It removed its in-app voice and video calling feature.

Use a Voice Note when tone genuinely helps and the match appears comfortable with that format. Keep the first one short. A long unsolicited monologue creates more work than connection.

## What Message Data Can Tell You

Message counts can describe your own activity, but Hinge's exported conversation data contains only your half. Without incoming messages, SwipeStats cannot prove that a match replied, was enthusiastic, ghosted, or agreed to a date.

A thread where you sent many messages could be a reciprocal conversation—or several unanswered messages. Any highlight should say exactly which observable proxy it uses.

## FAQ

### What should I say after “hey”?

Use one specific profile detail and ask a small question about it.

### How do I restart a dead conversation?

Send one relevant new topic or direct date suggestion. If there is no response, stop.

### How many messages before asking someone out?

There is no universal number. Ask when the exchange is reciprocal and both people have enough context to feel comfortable.

### Can Hinge tell me whether a conversation was good?

The export provides only your side, so it cannot establish the other person's sentiment or reply behavior by itself.
