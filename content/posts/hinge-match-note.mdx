## What the Hell Is a Hinge Match Note?

Hinge launched the Match Note feature on February 26, 2025, and it's one of those rare dating app updates that actually solves a real problem instead of just giving you another way to spend money.

Here's the deal. A **hinge match note** is a 255-character private note that gets shown to your matches after you both like each other. It's not on your public profile. Randos scrolling through your photos will never see it. Only people you've mutually matched with get the privilege of reading your innermost 255 characters.

And here's the part that makes it actually useful: the recipient **must acknowledge your note before they can start a conversation**. They can't just blow past it like they blow past your [carefully crafted prompts](/blog/best-hinge-prompts). They either read it and proceed, or they unmatch. There is no third option.

Hinge built this feature with TransTech Social Enterprises and Disability:IN, originally to help LGBTQIA+, neurodivergent, and disabled users share important context with matches. Things like gender identity, accessibility needs, or communication styles. But it's available to every single Hinge user (pun intended), and honestly, everyone should be using it.

Jackie Jantos, Hinge's CMO, called it something that "serves an important role for people from underrepresented groups while giving all daters a new way to connect more intentionally." Corporate-speak aside, she's right. It's a pre-screening tool disguised as a love letter. And it's an industry first, which means Hinge finally did something before Tinder could copy it.

## How to Set Up Your Hinge Match Note (It Takes 30 Seconds)

Setting this up is embarrassingly easy. You've spent longer deciding which [Hinge opening line](/blog/hinge-opening-lines) to send someone. Here's the walkthrough.

1. **Open Hinge** and tap your profile icon in the bottom right corner.
2. **Tap the pencil/edit icon** to enter profile editing mode.
3. **Scroll all the way down** past your prompts, photos, and whatever else you've curated to make yourself look interesting.
4. **Find the Match Note field** at the bottom. It's there. Keep scrolling.
5. **Type your note.** You have 255 characters. That's roughly two tweets from the pre-Elon era.
6. **Tap Done.**
7. **Toggle it to visible.** This makes it show up automatically for ALL new matches.

That's it. You can edit it or remove it any time. There's no commitment here, which is ironic given the context.

One thing to note: this note goes to every match. You can't customize it per person. So don't write "I love your dog" unless you're confident every single match has a dog in their profile. (They won't. Some of them have cats. Some of them have neither and that should concern you.)

## What to Actually Write in Your Hinge Match Note

This is where most people are going to screw it up. You have 255 characters to say something that makes a stranger want to keep talking to you. No pressure at all.

Here are some approaches that actually work, organized by what you're trying to accomplish.

### Relationship Goals & Intentions

If you're tired of matching with people who want completely different things (and then finding out four dates in), lead with what you're looking for.

- *"Looking for something serious. Not here to be your text buddy for 3 months."*
- *"Sober, but you don't have to be. Always down for a coffee first date."*
- *"Want a relationship, not a situationship. I've done that tour already."*

Direct. Clear. Saves everyone the "so what are you looking for" conversation that happens on date one and feels like a job interview.

### Lifestyle & Compatibility

This is where you share the stuff that's true about you that might actually affect whether someone wants to date you. You know, the real stuff that [your bio](/blog/hinge-bio) doesn't cover.

- *"ADHD brain here. I might text you 47 messages in a row and then disappear for 3 days. It's not personal."*
- *"Early bird. If your idea of morning is noon, we might have a scheduling conflict."*
- *"I work nights. If you need a 'good morning' text before 2pm, I'm not your person."*

These are genuinely useful pieces of information that prevent mismatched expectations down the road. Your future self will thank you for the awkward conversations you avoided.

### Dealbreakers (Framed Like a Human, Not a Bouncer)

There's a right way and a wrong way to share dealbreakers. The right way sounds like a person. The wrong way sounds like a nightclub door policy.

- *"I have kids and they're my world. If that's a dealbreaker, no hard feelings."*
- *"I'm ENM and upfront about it. If that's not your thing, all good."*
- *"I'm a homebody. If you need someone who wants to go out every weekend, we're going to clash."*

See how those all end with an off-ramp? That's the move. State your thing, acknowledge that it's not for everyone, and let people self-select. Much better than "NO DRAMA NO GAMES NO TIME-WASTERS" which, ironically, is always written by the most dramatic person on the app.

### The Quirky, Self-Aware Route

If your situation doesn't require a serious disclaimer, you can use this space to show some personality. Just make sure it actually sounds like you and not like a motivational poster at a dentist's office.

- *"My love language is sending memes at 2am. You've been warned."*
- *"I will judge you by your Spotify Wrapped. Just being honest."*
- *"I talk about my cat like she's my child. This is non-negotiable."*

These work because they're specific, slightly self-deprecating, and they give the other person something to respond to. Which is more than I can say for 90% of Hinge conversations.

## Hinge Match Note Examples That Don't Suck (and Some That Do)

Let me save you some time and embarrassment. Here are examples on both sides of the quality spectrum.

### The Good Ones (and Why They Work)

- **"Wheelchair user. Not looking for pity or inspiration porn. Looking for someone who can keep up with my trash talk."** Works because it's honest, funny, and immediately filters out anyone who'd be weird about it.
- **"I'm trans. If you're cool with that, let's chat. If not, the unmatch button is right there."** Direct. No apologies. Saves the author from the world's most exhausting conversation.
- **"Divorced dad of two. They come first. I come second. You come third. If that math works for you, let's talk."** Clear hierarchy. Specific. A little funny. Everything a good Match Note should be.
- **"I sign. If you know ASL, bonus points. If not, I'm patient and good at texting."** Practical information delivered with warmth.

### The Bad Ones (and Why They're Bad)

- **"Hey!"** Congratulations, you used 4 of your 255 characters to say absolutely nothing. This is the equivalent of wearing a blank t-shirt to a costume party.
- **"Looking for my person."** So is literally every human on a dating app. This tells your match nothing about you that they couldn't have guessed by the fact that you have a Hinge profile.
- **"NO smokers, NO drug users, NO liars, NO cheaters, NO time-wasters, NO drama."** You sound like a bouncer who hates their job. Nobody reads a wall of "NOs" and thinks "wow, they seem fun."
- **"Live, laugh, love."** I will find you and I will report your profile. (I won't, but I'll think about it.)

If your Match Note could be posted as an Instagram caption by a lifestyle influencer, start over. Be specific. Be yourself. Be the kind of person who says something worth reading.

## What Happens When Someone Reads Your Match Note

So you've written your masterpiece. All 255 characters of it. Now what?

Here's how it works from the other person's perspective. After you both [like each other on Hinge](/blog/how-to-like-someone-on-hinge) and a match is formed, your match sees a screen with your note on it. They have two options:

1. **Acknowledge it and start a conversation.** They read your note, they're cool with it, and now they can message you. The note basically served as a pre-conversation handshake.
2. **Unmatch.** They read your note, decided it's not going to work, and bounced.

That's it. There's no "maybe later" button. No "interesting, tell me more." It's a binary checkpoint. In or out.

And honestly? That's the best part. If someone reads your note about having kids, or being sober, or being polyamorous, and they still message you, you already know they're at least okay with it. You've skipped the most tedious part of online dating: the slow reveal of incompatibilities three weeks in.

Yes, some people will unmatch when they read your note. That's not a bug. It's the feature working exactly as intended. Every unmatch from your Match Note is a bad date you didn't have to go on. You're welcome.

## Does a Hinge Match Note Actually Help? (What the Data Says)

I know what you're thinking. "Cool feature, but does it actually do anything?" Fair question. Let's look at the numbers.

Hinge ran a beta test with over 2,000 users before launching this feature. Here's what they found:

- **68% said it improved their ability to assess compatibility.** That's more than two-thirds of testers saying "yeah, this helped me figure out if someone was right for me." Before even sending a message.
- **66% said it helped them be more authentic** on the app. Because apparently, people need a private note to feel comfortable being honest. Which says more about [how dating apps work](/blog/how-to-use-hinge) than about the feature itself.
- **83% of trans users responded positively** to the feature. Makes sense. Being able to share your identity in a private, controlled way before a conversation starts is genuinely valuable.
- **70% of queer users said it improved compatibility** assessment.

Now, I should note: this is Hinge's own data from their own study. They're not exactly going to publish results that say "our new feature is useless." Take it with the appropriate grain of salt.

But even if you cut those numbers in half, a Match Note is still doing passive filtering for you while you're asleep, at work, or doom-scrolling on a different app. Dating coach Rachel Helms called it the "secret sauce" for pre-screening matches. I wouldn't go that far (it's 255 characters, not a background check), but it's a useful tool that costs you nothing except 30 seconds of setup time.

## When You Probably Shouldn't Use a Match Note

Look, I just spent most of this article telling you to use this feature. But there are times when a Match Note does more harm than good.

**When your profile already says it.** If your Hinge prompts already mention that you're a dog person who loves hiking and wants something serious, don't repeat it in your Match Note. Redundancy is boring. Your match doesn't need to read the same information twice. They're already on a dating app. Their attention span is shot.

**When it's just a list of "NOs."** I touched on this above but it deserves repeating. Nobody swipes right on a restraining order. If your entire Match Note is a list of things you don't want, you haven't told your match anything about who you are. Only about who you're mad at.

**When you're overthinking it.** It's 255 characters. Not a thesis defense. Not a cover letter. Not a toast at your best friend's wedding. If you've been staring at the text field for more than five minutes, just write something real and move on. You can always edit it later. (And you will. Probably several times. That's fine.)

**When you're using it as a second bio.** The Match Note is for things your profile doesn't cover. If you're just restating your interests and hobbies, you're using a private feature to do a public feature's job.

## FAQ

### How do Hinge notes work?

When you set up a Hinge Match Note, it's a 255-character private message that only appears after two people mutually like each other. Your match sees the note and must acknowledge it before the conversation can begin. They can also choose to unmatch instead. It's automatic for all new matches once you toggle it on. Think of it as a velvet rope with a bouncer who hands out pamphlets about you.

### Are Hinge notes personalized?

No. Your Match Note is the same message for every match. You write it once, and it goes out to everyone you match with. So keep it universal enough that it applies broadly, but specific enough that it actually says something about you. If you want to send personalized messages to someone before matching, that's a different feature. Check out our guide on how to like someone on Hinge for tips on writing those.

### Can you see a match note again on Hinge?

The Match Note appears as a one-time acknowledgment screen after matching. Once you acknowledge it and move into the conversation, Hinge doesn't show it again in the chat. If your [match disappeared](/blog/hinge-match-disappeared), the note went with them. And if your hinge match note disappeared along with the match, that means they unmatched you. Sorry. Take a screenshot if you want to remember what it said. We live in the future. Act like it.

### How do I change my match note on Hinge?

Go to your profile, tap the edit icon, scroll to the bottom where your Match Note lives, and just rewrite it. Hit Done. That's it. Your updated note will apply to all future matches. Already-existing matches who already acknowledged the old note won't see the new one. So if you fix a typo after 50 people already read the version with "your" instead of "you're," just know that those 50 people judged you. Silently.

### Do Hinge voice notes disappear?

Voice notes and Match Notes are completely different features. Voice notes are audio messages you send in a conversation. Match Notes are text-based private notes shown to all your matches before chatting. Voice notes behave like regular messages in your chat. They don't disappear (unless the match itself disappears, which is a whole different problem).

### What's the difference between a Match Note and a voice note?

A Match Note is a 255-character text message displayed to all your matches before they can message you. A voice note is an audio message you send during a conversation with a specific person. Match Note = pre-conversation filter that applies to everyone. Voice note = mid-conversation audio clip sent to one person. They serve completely different purposes, and confusing them is like confusing your resume with a voicemail. Related, sure. But very different documents.
