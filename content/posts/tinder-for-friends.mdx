### TL;DR for the Friendless

Look, if you're Googling "tinder for friends," things have gotten bad. Not judging. Okay, judging a little. But here's what you need to know.

- Tinder doesn't have a friend feature. Tinder Social died years ago and nobody noticed.
- "Looking for friends" in your Tinder bio fools exactly zero people. You know it. They know it.
- Bumble BFF is the closest thing to a platonic dating app. Same swipe mechanics, same conversation graveyard.
- The friendship app market hit **$16 million in US spending in 2025** with **4.3 million downloads**. People are literally paying to find someone to eat lunch with.
- It takes **200 hours of shared time** to form one close friendship. Apps can introduce you. They can't force you to show up.

## You Can't Use Tinder for Friends (But People Keep Trying)

Let me save you the experiment. Tinder is not, has never been, and will never be a friend-making app.

Tinder Social launched in 2016 as a group feature that let you and your friends swipe on other friend groups. It was supposed to be the social future of dating. It lasted about as long as your New Year's resolution to go to the gym. RIP to a feature nobody used, mourned by absolutely no one.

Writing "looking for friends" in your Tinder bio is the dating app equivalent of reading Playboy "for the articles." Everyone sees through it. You're either in a relationship and testing the waters, freshly dumped and not ready to admit it, or genuinely looking for friends on the worst possible platform for that. No matter which one it is, people are still going to try to hook up with you.

Tinder's not completely ignoring the social angle. Their Double Date mode (launched June 2025) lets you swipe as a pair with a friend. Women were **3x more likely to Like a pair** than a solo profile, and users sent **35% more messages** in group chats. But it's still dating. You're just doing it with a wingman now.

The Tinder Sparks 2026 roadmap promises events, video speed dating, and more "modes." Still zero friend features. Because Tinder knows what it is. A [dating app](/blog/tinder-review). Always has been, always will be.

## The Loneliness Numbers Hit Harder Than Your Ex's Unfollow

Before you feel weird about wanting a friend app, let's look at how bad things actually are out there.

**58% of US adults feel lonely** (Cigna, 2021). Not "a little isolated." Lonely. That's more than half the country sitting at home wondering why nobody texted them back.

And it gets worse:

- **21% of Americans have no close friends at all** (YouGov). Zero. None.
- **61% of young adults (18-34) report loneliness.** The generation that grew up with unlimited social media has the fewest real friends. Poetic, really.
- **1 in 4 people globally** experience significant loneliness (Meta-Gallup, 2023).
- **15% of men have no close friends.** And **1 in 4 men under 30** have no close connections whatsoever.
- The US Surgeon General declared loneliness a **public health crisis** in 2023. Not a trend piece. A crisis. Like, the same guy who puts warnings on cigarette boxes.

Social isolation increases premature mortality risk by the same amount as smoking 15 cigarettes a day. It raises dementia risk by **50%**. Your loneliness is literally killing you. No pressure.

Here's the stat that should terrify every app developer in the friend space: it takes **200 hours of shared time** to form a close friendship (University of Kansas, 2018). That's 200 hours. Not 200 swipes. Not 200 "we should totally hang out sometime" messages that go nowhere. Two hundred actual hours in the same room as another human being.

How did we get here? The short version: after WWII, the nuclear family became everything. Friendships got demoted from "essential support system" to "nice to have." We put all our emotional eggs in one romantic basket and then acted shocked when that basket got heavy. Your partner was never supposed to be your best friend, therapist, gym buddy, and travel companion all at once. But here we are.

## Is There a Tinder for Friends? (Yeah, Kind Of)

If you're looking for an app like Tinder but for friends, [Bumble BFF](/blog/bumble-statistics) is the answer most people land on.

Whitney Wolfe Herd launched it in 2016 as a feature inside the Bumble app. Same swiping. Same 24-hour window to message. Same interface you already know from judging strangers by their photos. September 2025, they relaunched it with a group focus because apparently one-on-one friend dates are too much pressure for people who can barely manage one-on-one romantic dates.

The numbers are telling. **10-15% of Bumble's total engagement** comes from BFF. Revenue from the standalone BFF app? Literally $0. Bumble runs it as a loss leader because friendship doesn't monetize as well as loneliness and horniness combined.

The core problem with Bumble BFF is the same problem with every friend app: people swipe, match, send one message that says "omg we have so much in common!!" and then never speak again. Sound familiar? Our analysis of [Tinder data](/blog/tinder-statistics) shows the same pattern. People match and ghost. Turns out the flaw isn't the app. It's us.

But it does work sometimes. **2/3 of Gen Z** say they've met a friend online (Bumble survey, 2023). So the concept isn't dead. The execution just requires more effort than most people are willing to put in.

## 7 Best Friend-Making Apps That Aren't Tinder (Ranked by a Recovering Swipe Addict)

I've tested more apps to make friends than any grown man should admit to. Here's what's actually worth your time and what's a waste of storage on your phone.

### 1. Bumble BFF

The default. The McDonald's of friend-making apps. You already know how it works because it's the same app you used to find your last situationship, just with a different tab selected.

Swipe right on people who seem cool. Get 24 hours to message. Watch most conversations die after three messages. Repeat.

The free version is fine for testing the waters. The paid version filters out the tourists who downloaded it out of curiosity and never planned to actually meet anyone. If you're serious about this, paying actually helps (which is something I almost never say about [dating app subscriptions](/blog/is-tinder-gold-worth-it)).

**Best for:** People who already know how swiping works (so, everyone reading this).

### 2. Meetup

**60 million members.** Free. And it works through the one mechanism that actually produces lasting friendships: getting you in the same room with the same people over and over again.

While every other app on this list tries to solve the introduction problem, Meetup solves the 200-hour problem. You join a hiking group. You show up every Saturday. After a few months, those strangers become friends. Not because an algorithm matched you, but because you both kept showing up.

It's not sexy. It's not gamified. It looks like it was designed in 2008 (because it was). But it works better than anything else on this list, and it's not close.

**Best for:** People who can leave their house.

### 3. Timeleft

Weekly algorithmically-matched dinner parties in **250+ cities**. You sign up. You show up Wednesday night. You eat dinner with five strangers. That's it. That's the app.

**60% of users are women.** Majority are in their 30s and 40s. The group dynamic removes the pressure that kills one-on-one friend dates. Nobody's staring at you across a coffee table wondering if you're going to be their new best friend. You're just having dinner.

I love the concept because it forces the thing people are worst at: actually showing up in person.

**Best for:** People who are tired of one-on-one "friend dates" that feel like job interviews.

### 4. Friender

Integrates Spotify and OpenAI for interest matching. No swiping. It tries to match you based on actual compatibility rather than whether you look fun in your profile photo.

The music integration is genuinely clever. If someone's top artists overlap with yours by 80%, you probably have enough in common to survive a coffee. Probably.

**Best for:** Music snobs who want friends who also think Radiohead peaked with Kid A.

### 5. We3

Groups you into trios based on personality and values. Instead of the pressure of a one-on-one friend date (which, let's be honest, feels exactly like a regular date minus the possibility of making out), you meet two people at once. The group dynamic takes the edge off.

**Best for:** Introverts who find one-on-one friend dates as terrifying as actual dates.

### 6. Peanut

**5 million users.** Built specifically for mothers. If you've ever spent an entire week where your longest conversation was with a toddler about why the sky is blue, this app exists for you.

It connects moms at similar stages (pregnancy, newborn, toddler, school-age) so you can find someone who understands why you haven't slept in three years.

**Best for:** Parents who need adult conversation that doesn't involve Bluey.

### 7. Hey! VINA

Women only. Available in **158 countries.** The vibe is warmer and less transactional than Bumble BFF. The prompts are better. The user base is smaller but more intentional.

Think of it as the [Hinge of friend apps](/blog/hinge-vs-bumble). Fewer users, higher quality interactions. At least in theory.

**Best for:** Women who want a girls-only space without dudes sliding into friendship requests.

## Why Friend Apps Fail Just As Hard As Dating Apps

I know you were hoping for a magic bullet. An app that would deliver a best friend to your door like DoorDash delivers pad thai. Bad news: friend apps have the exact same problems as dating apps, and a few new ones.

**Swipe fatigue transfers.** If you're already burned out from swiping on [Tinder](/blog/tinder-algorithm), adding more swiping (but for friends!) is not the cure. It's like switching from vodka to tequila and calling it sobriety. Your thumbs are tired. Your dopamine receptors are fried. More swiping is not the answer.

**The flakiness problem is worse.** People match and never meet. Our data from analyzing **7,000+ Tinder profiles and 294 million swipes** shows the average match-to-message rate is already depressingly low on dating apps. Friend apps are worse because the urgency is lower. Nobody's horny for friendship. There's no biological drive pushing you to meet that cool person who also likes hiking and true crime podcasts.

**Paying actually helps.** I know that sounds like a scam, but hear me out. Free users on Bumble BFF include tons of tourists who signed up because they were curious and will never message anyone. Premium filters them out. You want to be in the pool with people who cared enough to pay, not the people who downloaded it between TikTok scrolls.

**The 200-hour barrier is real.** An app can introduce you to someone. It cannot make you show up every week. It cannot make you text first. It cannot make you vulnerable enough to turn an acquaintance into an actual friend. That's on you. And honestly? That's the part most people fail at. Not the finding. The keeping.

## How to Actually Make Friends Without an App (Controversial, I Know)

You've read this far, which means you're either genuinely lonely or procrastinating. Either way, here's what actually works.

**Recurring activities beat one-off meetups every time.** A weekly basketball league. A monthly book club. A co-working space you show up to three times a week. The 200-hour rule means you need repeated exposure to the same people. One coffee date with a stranger from Bumble BFF is not going to cut it. You need to see the same faces over and over until familiarity turns into friendship. It's not romantic. It's science.

**The 3-message rule.** If you match with someone on a friend app, suggest meeting within three messages. If they dodge, move on. You're not here to collect pen pals. The people who actually want friends will say yes. The people who wanted a dopamine hit from matching will disappear. Let them.

**Say yes to everything for 30 days.** That weird pottery class? Go. Your coworker's birthday drinks? Show up. The improv show nobody wants to attend? Be there. You cannot make friends from your couch. I've tried. It doesn't work. (Believe me, I've spent entire weekends trying to will friendships into existence through a phone screen. All I got was eye strain and a vague sense of shame.)

**The infrastructure already exists.** Sports leagues. Volunteering. Hobby classes. Co-working spaces. Religious communities if that's your thing. Language exchange meetups. Running clubs. The friend-making opportunities are out there. They just require you to leave your apartment, which I know is the part you were hoping to avoid when you Googled "tinder for friends."

Look, if you're reading a **3,000-word article** about how to make friends, you already know what the problem is. And the problem isn't that the right app doesn't exist. The problem is that friendship in adulthood requires the one thing none of us want to do: show up consistently, be vulnerable, and accept that not every attempt will work out.

But some will. And one good friendship is worth more than every match you've ever gotten on any app.

Now close this tab and go text someone you haven't talked to in a while. They're probably just as lonely as you are.

## FAQ

### Can you use Tinder for friends?

Technically, nothing stops you from writing "just looking for friends" in your [Tinder bio](/blog/best-tinder-bios). But people will still treat it like a dating app because that's what it is. You'll spend most of your time explaining that no, you really do just want to be friends. It's exhausting and ineffective.

### What does "looking for new friends" mean on Tinder?

It usually means one of three things: they're in a relationship and exploring, they just got out of a relationship and aren't ready to date, or they actually want friends but chose the worst platform imaginable. Your guess is as good as theirs.

### Is Bumble BFF free?

Yes, the basic version is free. Premium (Bumble Premium) gives you better filters and removes the 24-hour messaging window. Paying is worth it if you're serious. If you're just testing, free is fine.

### What's the best friend-making app for men?

Bumble BFF or Meetup. Most other friend apps skew heavily female. [Men need friends too](/blog/how-to-get-more-matches-on-tinder-as-a-man) (arguably more, given that **15% of men have zero close friends**), but the app market hasn't caught up yet. Meetup works well because it's activity-based and doesn't require you to write a "friend bio," which most guys would rather chew glass than do.

### Is there an app like Tinder but for friends?

Bumble BFF is the closest thing. Same swiping, same matching, same potential for conversations that go nowhere. The friendship app market hit **$16 million in US spending** and **4.3 million downloads** in 2025, so there are plenty of options. But the mechanics are all basically the same: see profile, swipe, match, message, meet (maybe).
